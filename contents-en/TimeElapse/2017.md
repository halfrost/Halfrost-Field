## Technological Change Has Acceleration

![](http://upload-images.jianshu.io/upload_images/1194012-15efb9e22d528a7f.png?imageMogr2/auto-orient/strip%7CimageView2/2/w/1240)

Starting in 2010, which was defined as the first year of the mobile Internet, mobile development gradually became popular. I joined this wave after graduation. It is said that when mobile development was booming, even barbers could get a monthly salary of 10–20K after a few months of training. That shows just how hungry the market was for mobile talent at the time. But starting around the end of 2014, hiring requirements for mobile development returned to a more rational level and gradually became higher. Today, large companies basically no longer recruit mobile developers below senior level through experienced-hire channels. Interviews are, of course, much harder than they were a few years ago. BAT interviews may examine frontier technologies, hotfix and cross-platform solutions, low-level technologies, LLVM + Clang, fundamentals, WebKit, and JSCore. Some iOS developers around me have also gradually switched to writing JavaScript. Domestic iOS developers may also feel the impact of the arrival of the “big frontend” era on their own technical stack. (Of course, overseas iOS developers are not particularly interested in these things; overseas development still tends to be native.) Returning to the domestic market, as some big-frontend technologies gradually encroached on areas previously handled by iOS developers, perhaps before everyone had even become familiar with or proficient in all kinds of frontend frameworks, AI suddenly appeared in everyone’s field of view. Concepts such as machine learning and deep learning came rushing in like a tide.

From the end of 2012 to the beginning of 2013, a large number of startups sprang up like mushrooms after rain. By the end of 2014, many companies did not survive that winter. In 2017, large numbers of startups were still being born and then collapsing, such as the various bike-sharing companies. The capital market is just that cruel.

Some colleagues around me have also become anxious. To be honest, I have been anxious too. Hot technologies seem to change almost every year. At the beginning of the year, you may not yet have mastered blockchain and the three major frontend frameworks; by the end of the year, AI immediately crushes everything again. A frontend expert once explained it to me like this: “Technology has to be followed constantly. In frontend, if you do not keep up with new technologies for a few months, they may look unfamiliar when you see them. If you stick with old technologies for years without changing, hehe, by the time you try to understand the new technologies again, the frontend framework landscape may already have completely changed.” This answer may be somewhat exaggerated, but it also reflects from another angle just how fast frontend has developed over the past few years.

You can also think back on the technological changes of recent years. You may also feel that **technological change has acceleration**.


## Where Does Anxiety Come From?

I will still use iOS developers as an example. Three images often appear in iOS developer groups.


![](http://upload-images.jianshu.io/upload_images/1194012-8ff5c112f5ae8169.png?imageMogr2/auto-orient/strip%7CimageView2/2/w/1240)


One is “nobody wants iOS developers anymore,” and the other is “people want iOS developers again.” These two images often appear as memes in various discussion groups. Why? Because part of the demand for iOS development has been taken over by frontend developers. Many small companies in China directly write JS, RN, Weex, and similar technologies into their job requirements. This directly leads to iOS developers who do not know JS becoming unwanted. Whenever Apple “cracks down” on cross-platform or hotfix approaches, or takes any related action, native developers flood the chat with the “iOS developers are wanted again” expression. Frontend developers may have similar anxiety. Their anxiety may come from how well they have mastered the three major frameworks. If you are proficient in only one framework and encounter candidates who are proficient in all three when looking for a job, you may also feel insecure.

This is one source of anxiety: not knowing certain knowledge points may lead to being unable to find a job, or unable to find a good job.

Another anxiety may come from “backflow,” perhaps?

Hot technologies change extremely quickly every year. For example, AI became so popular this year that fresh graduates who received AI offers could directly and ruthlessly out-compensate people with two to three years of work experience. Even people with two to three years of experience who have been keeping up with these new technologies will feel pressure. That is why the second image above exists: please stop learning; we cannot keep up.

After all, very few people can guarantee large blocks of time every day to study new technologies. Besides completing company work and possible overtime, people are tired when they get home and need rest. There is not much large-block time available every day for learning new technologies.

Different people also learn at different speeds and reach different levels of mastery. After comparing yourself with others, you may find that you learn more slowly and learn less. If you force this comparison, it will indeed create anxiety that you are not as good as others.

## How Can We Learn Efficiently?

Given how fast technology changes, how should we learn efficiently to stay competitive and avoid being eliminated? After nearly ten months of growth, I now have some right to speak on this question. Please allow me to slowly walk through my experiences and insights from this year.

I will start with mindset, and finally discuss my learning methods. Following this line of thought, I hope readers can gain something from it.

### 1. View Anxiety and Confusion Correctly

Once programmers become anxious or confused, their sense of achievement drops significantly. If this continues for a long time, it leads to insufficient motivation. But as analyzed above, the real causes of anxiety objectively exist. So how should we face them?

This is how I face them.

In the process of technological change, if you blindly chase new technologies, have you ever thought about what the purpose of chasing them is? Is it to change jobs or transfer roles? Or is it to increase compensation, realize life goals, and achieve self-worth? Both reasons are valid. The point to pay attention to is not to blindly chase new technologies. Blindly chasing them will only eventually reduce you to a slave of technology. We need to be masters of technology and face technological changes more calmly.

How should you choose? If you are not changing roles and do not have a clear goal, learning knowledge that may not be used in your current job can be somewhat “blind.” The learning process for this knowledge may also be inefficient. When inefficient learning meets someone else’s rapid growth, comparison creates new anxiety. I have firsthand experience with this.

My suggestion here is still to prioritize learning the knowledge used in your company’s projects and dig deep into technical pain points. Use spare time to broaden horizontally into other areas. Like a T-shaped talent, first specialize in one area, then expand breadth. If you pursue breadth first without depth, you may end up unable even to find a job.


Now let us talk about confusion. There are two sources of confusion: first, not seeing your value to the company; second, not seeing the path for your future development.


First, the first source of confusion: not seeing your own value. Many people write business code at the company every day, commonly called “moving bricks.” They do it every day and feel like they are not improving at all. Looking back at people in framework departments or researchers in their own departments, they are studying or building frameworks or tools every day. When many people use their work, they get a strong sense of achievement. They also believe that business code has no technical depth, and that the logic and processes in business code provide no advantage once they switch to another company. This way of thinking is actually wrong; it is incorrect and one-sided. How to correctly understand and change this view will be discussed in more detail in the next section.

Now let us talk about the second source of confusion: not seeing where the future path lies. I think this confusion comes from an unclear self-positioning. A teacher once told me that in the first five years after graduation, you can choose among various kinds of development: frontend, backend, or mobile. You can choose freely. What is this for? It is to find the direction that you are interested in, that matches your strengths, and that you intend to keep doing for the rest of your life. If you still cannot see your future development path, consider broadening your vision and looking for the direction you are truly interested in. By “truly interested,” I mean something like staying up late to play games without feeling sleepy. If, in some direction, you can keep coding late into the night or even all night without being forced by the company to work overtime and without getting tired of it, then that must be something you are interested in. Once the direction is determined, you will no longer be confused. Run toward the goal.

### 2. How Should You Choose Between Business and Architecture?

Among programmers, there may be a chain of contempt where those who write architecture look down on those who write business code. This kind of contempt is biased.

First, the revenue of the vast majority of companies comes from the company’s business, with only a very small number of exceptions. Students who write business code do not need to feel that business code has no value. You should understand that the business code you write makes money for the company.

Of course, students who can write internal frameworks or tools in a company must have accumulated a certain depth of technical expertise. Frameworks and tools do not appear out of nowhere. They are born to solve problems or pain points: either to solve development pain points or to improve development efficiency. Ask yourself: without a certain understanding and awareness of development, how can one deeply understand and feel these pain points? If you do not understand them, you cannot build frameworks that solve problems or tools that are useful and easy to use.

I believe that people who can write frameworks or tools must have a certain amount of technical accumulation, and must be able to understand and see clearly some pain points in development or business. They then develop things that solve these problems.


At least for most companies in China today, business developers are indispensable. For survival, small companies can lack framework and tool developers, but they cannot lack business developers. Large companies need business developers even more. At present, there do not seem to be companies in China that do not write business code at all. If a company purely writes frameworks or tools for other companies to use and makes money from that, then those frameworks and tools become that company’s business.


Now let us talk about my understanding of architects or higher-level titles.

In our BU, there is a dedicated architecture group, and everyone in it is at least a P7 architect. What is their work? They provide solutions that can solve current development pain points. I have communicated with them. Although they do not understand the business down to every line of code word for word, they have an overall understanding of the company’s entire business process and data flow. Their understanding of the business is deeper and broader than ours, since we are responsible for only a single business area. On top of that, they can solve difficulties and pain points in development, and they can also contribute their own thinking to the business development of the BU. Architects also have the ability to independently discover business value.


Perhaps everyone thinks architects do not write code, but they need to produce solutions. To some extent, producing solutions is even harder than writing code. Where does the inspiration for solutions come from? It comes from a deep understanding of the company’s business and a deep accumulation of technical expertise. Without business understanding, the proposed solution may not fully fit the company. Without deep technical accumulation, the performance of the proposed solution may not be optimal.


I think everyone should not look down on the business-code part. If you are a programmer writing business code in a company, please do not give up on yourself. When the company assigns a business area to you, whether you can deliver it on time and efficiently is the issue you need to consider right now. Once you are very fluent with the current business and have a deep understanding of it, you can then consider understanding the product line you are in and the process of this business line. While understanding the process, also think about why the company’s current architecture is designed this way. What are its advantages and disadvantages? What can be changed and what cannot? Which issues are caused by historical legacy?


Only with this kind of accumulation over time, after you have accumulated a lot of business experience and excellent, reasonable architecture designs across many business scenarios, can you move toward becoming an architect.


I firmly believe one thing: when a company pays to hire an architect, the job responsibility must be to design and complete certain architecture-related work for the company. As the saying goes, there is no best architecture, only the most suitable architecture. The value of architects lies precisely here: after deeply understanding the company’s business requirements, they tailor the best and most suitable architecture for the company’s business.

At the company, a P8 senior algorithm expert sits next to my workstation. Before I knew his title, I observed that his daily work was to put various algorithms into production and actually solve pain points and development difficulties inside the company. Whenever product managers or developers came to ask him about some piece of logic, he knew it like the back of his hand. For a while, I thought all the logic they asked about was logic he had personally participated in developing. Only after I later learned his title did I more deeply feel the value of an algorithm expert.

In non-academic industrial production, the value of an algorithm expert lies in using various algorithms to solve diverse business pain points in the company. I saw him use various graph algorithms plus machine learning to solve intelligent scheduling problems in real production. His understanding of the business he was responsible for was extremely deep. (Of course, in academic research, the value of an algorithm expert should lie in creating algorithms or improving algorithm efficiency.)


In the iOS field, everyone also knows Brother Di. Brother Di is an architect. After communicating with him, I learned that his daily work includes organizing departmental personnel, making decisions on technical selection, organizing business, and producing architecture solutions. Therefore, I believe that **the work of an architect is based on solving business problems and driving business growth; on top of that, it improves the company’s architecture so that it can provide better performance externally, while also requiring the ability to coordinate people, technology, and business**.


A good product must be able to perfectly solve user pain points. Understanding and discovering business value is also an ability. At least, this ability is essential for architects. I am also currently training myself in business. While deeply understanding the company’s business, I am also thinking about how to design the architecture, why it should be designed this way, and whether there is a better design. I cannot say that I will definitely become an architect in the future, but at least I believe I am working in the direction of becoming one.


### 3. Differences and Unity in Technical Division of Labor

![](http://upload-images.jianshu.io/upload_images/1194012-c93b4a9acef372d4.jpeg?imageMogr2/auto-orient/strip%7CimageView2/2/w/1240)

This year, I have been involved in both frontend and backend work. The frontend can be considered the layer closest to users. And now we are in the big-frontend era; you may find that frontend can do more and more work. Moving forward, it can touch the client; moving backward, it can touch Nginx. The frontend engineer’s tech stack has become extremely broad. Backend engineers, by contrast, may seem less busy than frontend engineers.

Frontend data comes from the backend. The frontend focuses more on UI, interaction, and design. The backend focuses more on API performance and data correctness. But as cloud infrastructure gradually improves, backend infrastructure has moved to the cloud, making configuration and management in this area exceptionally easy. All of this is handed over to the cloud.

Frontend frameworks and browsers are now developing by leaps and bounds. In business, part of the logic is handled directly on the frontend. Even with frontend-backend separation today, the frontend is taking on more and more roles inside companies. There is a joke: a big-frontend engineer says to a backend engineer, “Isn’t your backend just writing APIs and spitting out strings?” The backend engineer says to the big-frontend engineer, “Isn’t your frontend just assembling pages? The frontend is just a pretty string.” Although neither statement is accurate, they do abstract the content of their work from another angle.

So frontend and backend have different divisions of labor, but they are still in a cooperative relationship, and neither can be missing.

In the private group of Zhu Yun, a female PhD at Airbnb, I saw a Q&A:

>Question: As a backend developer, I would like to ask Sister An how to improve my ability to handle complex business logic, design ability, and abstraction ability. If I take over a system with insufficient stability, how should I reduce complexity and gradually improve stability?

Teacher Zhu Yun gave a very concise and excellent answer:

>To control something, you must first know it thoroughly. Understand the ins and outs of all business logic, know some typical system design solutions and the problems they target, as well as their pros and cons. Have exposure to real systems. On top of this, it becomes possible to apply an appropriate design to the current business logic and abstract the existing business logic into an existing (partial) solution, or a more classical approach.
>
>If you take over a system with poor stability and do not have enough time to redesign it from scratch and completely refactor it, then at least identify the key factors affecting stability, and then prioritize them according to importance, urgency, and the resources required to solve the problem (time, skill sets, people, etc.), tackling them one by one. For all improvement actions, make sure there is appropriate evaluation and monitoring, so that you can know what effect different measures actually have.


Combined with the architecture-business relationship we discussed earlier, the same thing is also unified. **Whether frontend or backend, whether business or architecture, the final goal of these divisions of labor is the same: let technology drive business growth and double company profitability**. Students who write frameworks or tools build better frameworks and tools to improve development efficiency. Students who write business code use technology to make business stability and logic more stable and reliable. The ultimate purpose is to drive the company’s business growth. I believe that once you see this point, you can recognize your own value in the company. When you find self-worth and realize company value, it becomes less easy to feel confused, and a sense of achievement becomes easier to obtain.

### 4. Efficient Learning Methods

At this point, I will use my experiences from this year to talk about what I believe efficient learning methods look like. I will also summarize my work and gains from this year along the way. There were roughly three stages.

First stage: the iOS stage

![](http://upload-images.jianshu.io/upload_images/1194012-208a5297dce805f3.png?imageMogr2/auto-orient/strip%7CimageView2/2/w/1240)

I came here in February this year, after the Spring Festival. After I joined the company, the first task assigned to me was to solve memory-leak-related problems in the project. Because the project used RAC throughout, and teammates did not have a deep enough understanding of its internal principles, it was easy to write code with memory leaks. After I joined, I found all the memory-leak-related issues in the group. Later, the group started componentization work, so I began researching and implementing a routing solution suitable for internal group use.

Later, the company was promoting a cross-platform solution internally — Weex. I was also honored to be assigned to research this area. By reading source code, I learned a great deal. This brings us to May of this year.

Second stage: the frontend stage

![](http://upload-images.jianshu.io/upload_images/1194012-6b6d7f942346d722.png?imageMogr2/auto-orient/strip%7CimageView2/2/w/1240)

After June, the group took on a new task, and I also proactively applied to write the frontend pages for it. Since I had already learned about Weex, choosing Vue as the framework was a natural next step. So I started writing Vue-related projects.

During just over two months of project iterations, I learned a lot. Usage of various internal scaffolding tools, frontend development processes, frontend page instrumentation, frontend release processes, continuous integration for frontend projects, page performance monitoring APM, frontend engineering, componentization... All of these also exist in iOS, but I encountered them all within just a few iterations. Compared with other students who began learning frontend around the same time, my progress was relatively “astonishing.” At least after I had learned the entire flow, he was still exploring JS.

Although my frontend experience is still shallow now, I can handle basic business iterations as a P4. I also deeply understand that frontend contains a huge amount of content, and a few months only scratches the surface of the surface. But at least it allowed me to experience daily frontend development.

I believe my frontend learning was efficient. Why was it efficient? Because I learned through practice. **Using real company projects to train yourself makes learning especially efficient**. For problems encountered in the project every day, I would purposefully search online for solutions while commuting on the subway. The learning goals were very clear. I improved much faster than students who only read syntax every day without practicing.


For getting started with frontend, I can provide my path.

First, of course, learn JavaScript and Css. The entry method is still reading books. I read these books:

After that comes a large amount of real-project training. Participate in the “beating” of company business iterations, and you will quickly learn many things. After a few iterations, you can run through the whole process.

Of course, to increase training intensity, I also practiced on Github myself. At the time, I was also very interested in the Electron framework, so I wrote this open-source project: [A Cross-Platform App for Three Platforms Developed with the Vue Ecosystem + Electron](https://github.com/halfrost/vue-objccn/blob/master/README.md).

Another master is Pengpeng, who joined the company on the same day as I did. He is also a frontend developer and very technically strong. I also have a very good relationship with him. I usually ask him frontend questions as well.

The last master is my college roommate. He is also very strong in frontend, and I also consult him when I do not understand something.

Here I also thank the three masters above for answering my questions and carrying a frontend beginner all the way forward.

To summarize this period of frontend learning: **a large amount of project practice + proactive and purposeful reading and learning + guidance from experts = efficient learning**.

Third stage: the Go stage

In July, I followed my team lead and switched to writing backend business code. In just a few months, I also grew very quickly. I now understand the basic usage of Go quite well. Besides reading books and studying on my own, whenever I encountered various technical problems in the project, my team lead and teammates also answered my questions. They also helped carry me forward. If I had learned and explored everything by myself, I do not know how long it would have taken to understand so many things above.

To summarize this period of backend learning, it is likewise: **a large amount of project practice + proactive and purposeful reading and learning + guidance from experts = efficient learning**.

The experiences above are relatively successful experiences. Compared with learning Haskell, the progress was obviously “astonishing.” While solving pain points in company projects, I also rapidly learned and improved a large amount of knowledge. Thinking back to what my team lead said before, **practicing in company projects is the fastest way to grow**. I also used one year to verify the truth of this statement.

### 5. Summary


How can programmers maintain a high growth rate amid successive waves of technological change? My answer is: the fastest way to grow is to practice within real company projects. First, business iteration schedules keep your learning from dragging on; you must complete assigned tasks within the schedule, which provides a strong guarantee for learning progress. Second, practice in real projects gives you hands-on experience with language proficiency, the language ecosystem, the development process, bug investigation workflows, monitoring, and many other areas. Real projects also expose you to all kinds of pitfalls; the more pitfalls you step into, the more you grow. **The right way to learn should also be to combine learning with concrete business scenarios, helping the company create value by using the technologies you have mastered to deliver business services**. Viewed this way, such growth is certainly the fastest.

A well-known computer science expert once advised programmers to grow at a pace of learning at least one unfamiliar language every year. Judging by this year's results, I accomplished that.

After working for several years, programmers should not limit their horizons solely to their own programming language. You can rise above programming languages, broaden your view, and think about problems from a higher level. Of course, to people at a higher level, the thoughts I have shared above may seem fairly ordinary. But people at higher levels must also have walked this path step by step, until eventually they could make broad judgments and have ideas that influence the entire industry. The above is some of the understanding I gained this year beyond technology itself. All of it comes from the “valuable experience” of my own hands-on practice. I hope readers who have made it this far can take something away from it.

Of course, everyone grows in different ways. I am merely offering one method that I have validated through practice and found feasible. If you already have a better method, you are very welcome to share it as well; you can simply treat this article as my year-end summary. If you also feel short on methods for growth, you might consider trying the methods described in this article. Let us encourage each other.

## Closing Thoughts


A year ago, a P9 senior engineer from Alibaba asked me a rather philosophical question: “As a programmer who has been developing software for so many years, how do you view software development?” At the time, I answered, “Are you asking about frontend development or iOS development?” He replied: “Can you avoid limiting yourself to programming languages? Talk from a macro perspective about your own understanding of the definition and process of development.” At that moment, I could not answer right away. It was not that I had nothing to say, but rather that I felt the question was too broad and hard to answer. Now, after working with different languages and development tasks across different roles, I have my own answer to this question. For questions like this, the depth of the answer may reveal the depth of a person's level. Without years of refinement in software development, and without sufficient breadth and depth of knowledge, the answer to such a question will inevitably be relatively superficial. It is like having already mastered advanced mathematics and then asking an elementary school student to solve a linear equation in one variable. No matter how they solve the equation or what method they use, you can see their level at a glance. Similarly, the question above is being asked by an expert standing at a higher vantage point. No matter what answer you give, based on his experience, he can roughly judge your level and experience from the depth of your answer.

I wonder whether readers who have reached this point already have a relatively perfect answer in mind. I plan to leave this question for next year as the topic of 2018's “Time Flies”.

Finally, I want to share a recent realization. There is a classic book, 《Clean Code》, whose Chinese title is The Way of Clean Code. Recently, I have been taking it out and flipping through it again, intentionally or unintentionally. When reading it for the second, third, or even fourth time, the experience is different every time. During the first read, perhaps because I lacked enough experience, there were not many places where I could resonate with the author. Most of the time, I was just reading the text and code in the book and trying to understand the author's intent. Of course, that is also the most basic level. But recently I found that when reading it for the second or third time, I could resonate much more with the author. That resonance comes from my own day-to-day development process: some of the examples in the book are things I have personally experienced, whether from refactoring a feature, racking my brain to design an architecture, or handling some special requirement. All kinds of personal experiences resurface in my mind. I feel that part of the book's content has already been internalized as my own. Of course, there is still another part that does not yet resonate.

Reading the same book over and over again brings different insights at different stages of a programmer's growth. At first, you may feel that the book is very thick and that the content is dry. But after you have gone through a lot, you will find that the book is actually quite thin. Looking back at it, the book becomes like a memoir of your own different stages, because you have personally experienced much of what it contains. Perhaps this is the path of growth in one's own level. It can also be considered the most direct material manifestation of personal growth.

All right, that is it for 2017's “Time Flies”. If you have any objections or anything you would like to discuss, feel free to reach out.
