|Tier|Term| Explanation| 
|:----:|:-----:|:-----|

|2| Top Student |A highly intelligent person hidden among ordinary people, with a broad social circle, strong adaptability, mastery of music, chess, calligraphy, and painting—polished, impressive, and high-class.|

### Exam Mindset

The TOEFL is a test of ability. It is not designed to make life difficult for everyone by throwing extremely hard or tricky questions at you (I only encountered that once in the TPOs). At the same time, the exam is not as hard as many people imagine. From talking with fellow test-takers, I found that after the exam reform, the margin for error is still quite generous. So when approaching the exam, do not fear it. Look down on it strategically, and believe that your effort will definitely translate into a corresponding score. At the same time, every exam has test-taking techniques. Whether through courses, other people’s guides, or your own takeaways during review, these are all ways to discover the exam’s patterns. You must develop a sense of familiarity with the TOEFL—a sense that “I know what kind of question you want to ask, and where you are going to ask it.” You need to anticipate the test writer’s anticipation. This requires a sincere heart during review. You need to understand the exam the way you would understand a girl: learn to anticipate what she is thinking, do what she wants you to do, and avoid wasting energy on irrelevant things—or courting disaster. So when reviewing, please make a plan, use techniques, focus your energy on the places most likely to produce results, and take every extra point you can get.

The TOEFL has four sections: reading, listening, speaking, and writing. In fact, all four share a common foundation: vocabulary. If your vocabulary is not good enough, a score of 100 is just a vague fantasy. So in the first month, you must calm down and focus on memorizing words properly. For vocabulary, use the KMF vocabulary app and Momo Word. I was working full-time, so I used spare fragments of time with a goal of 300 words per day. During the day, I tried to finish memorizing during lunch breaks and meals. Don’t expect to remember a word forever just because you memorized it once, because you will need to go through it 3–4 times. Memorizing vocabulary is actually an accelerating process. By the later rounds, the number of words you still need to memorize may be less than half, so there is no need to worry.

Listening is the final boost that takes you from 80 to 100. In fact, other than reading, every section is closely tied to listening. To score high in every section, you need to be able to accurately reflect the information you hear. I got 74 on my first attempt. I think a major reason, besides nerves during my first real exam and some unexpected incidents, was that I did not take listening seriously enough. I was not mentally prepared to listen carefully, so in the integrated speaking and writing tasks, I failed to hit all the scoring points. Therefore, everyone must attach importance to listening mentally, and remind yourself during the exam to maintain your listening focus. Stay alert at all times and look for the points where questions will be asked.


### Vocabulary

I memorized vocabulary entirely using fragmented time—for example, waiting for the bus to and from work, riding the subway, walking after lunch, and wandering around after dinner. All of these fragments of time can be used to memorize words. Do not spend five minutes memorizing a single word. The correct approach should be repeated exposure. Look at one word for 20 seconds and move on, then repeat it 3–4 more times during the day. The more repetitions you have, the more firmly you will remember it. Imagine this: scenario one, a stranger greets you and chats with you for 10 minutes. One year later, will you still remember him? Scenario two, a stranger greets you and chats with you for one second every day, meeting you every single day for 365 days. One year later, will you still remember him? Obviously, daily repetition will definitely make you remember him. Below is my word-review record from my vocabulary app:

### Time Planning

For TOEFL preparation, it is best to finish quickly and decisively. The longer you drag it out, the less efficient your preparation becomes. In general, a 3–4 month cycle is ideal.

- Month 1: foundation-building stage.

This stage is a preparation period: memorize vocabulary, aiming for 300 words per day. While memorizing words, you can start doing reading TPOs, practicing one set per day.

- Month 2: peak review stage.

Except for integrated writing and integrated speaking, you should allocate time to every section every day to complete your daily goals: continue 300 words per day, one reading TPO per day, one listening TPO per day, and one independent speaking question per day. This period is the most painful, because programmers are usually quite busy at work. People generally get home around 23:00, and if there is overtime, they may get home at 1–2 a.m. So before sleeping, you must properly arrange work, life, and study. Watching time slip away every day is maddening, but everyone must persist. For students, however, this workload should be relatively easy to complete.

- Month 3: sprint stage.

This is the stage where every question type needs to be pushed hard. The focus may shift to integrated speaking and writing. Continue 250 words per day, one reading TPO per day, one listening TPO per day, one independent speaking question per day, try to do one integrated speaking set per day (in my actual case, I did around 30 speaking sets before the exam), and one writing set every two days (I did 5 sets before the exam). As for the specific daily schedule, it varies from person to person. I can share my weekend schedule.

​		In the morning, no matter what time I got up, I did a mock test of one reading TPO and one listening TPO, then reviewed the mistakes.

​		In the afternoon, I took a nap, then did one writing set after waking up.

​		Before bed, I completed one speaking TPO set.

​		In between, I found time to memorize some vocabulary and some source materials (usable for both speaking and writing).

Some people think you need to do two TPO sets per day. I think that is very hard to achieve. After all, after getting questions wrong, you still need to check why you got them wrong. Basically, one TPO set per day is about right. Besides, TPO resources are limited and must not be wasted. You must reflect on each set after doing it. Next, I will talk separately about review methods for the four sections.


### Reading

Reading is a key area for scoring, and vocabulary is the foundation, so you must go through the words several times.

During review, learn to identify paraphrasing. Believe that in the actual exam, the vast majority of questions are still testing paraphrases of the original text. Quickly locate the keyword KEYWORD, compare the original passage against the question options, and use process of elimination for every question. This can basically ensure a very high score. In two of my exams, I had very little time left for the last three questions, yet my final score was 28. On one hand, this shows the correctness of the paraphrasing approach; on the other, it also shows the exam’s margin for error.

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/150_26.png)

After finishing each TPO reading passage, you can write a reflection: where exactly did you go wrong on the incorrect questions? Then read the passage intensively, checking long and difficult sentences as well as unfamiliar words. I read around 30 passages intensively. Finish all TPO reading before the exam, because it is possible for reading questions to be turned into listening questions. If you finish all TPOs, there should be no major problem in the end.

### Listening

Listening is the most important boost from 80 to 100. Listening questions have a margin for error. Based on my exam experience, each time I would encounter seven or eight questions where I needed to think for a bit and choose between two options. Both times, my listening score was 23, higher than I expected. The most important thing in listening review is to listen a lot.

1. By listening a lot, develop an ear for the sound structure of English.

Listening is not about hearing individual words one by one. Every sentence has its own subject-verb-object structure. The exam points are basically testing whether you heard the subject or object. A small number of questions test your understanding of the overall attitude or the attitude within a paragraph.

2. By listening a lot, become familiar with paragraph structure, and develop sensitivity to question points, questions, discourse markers, and transition words. This will help you raise your auditory attention right before the question points during the exam.

Do one TPO set every day, and after extra practice tests, do intensive listening. Intensive listening means listening while thinking about the two key points above, going through it once. In the two weeks before the exam, you should be able to do questions at 1.2x speed without problems. Your average review score will basically be your exam score. When doing questions, persist to the end. Do not give up just because you did not hear one sentence or one paragraph clearly. In many cases, listening questions can be answered even without fully understanding the listening material. But you need to develop the endurance and attention to listen to six questions in a row. TPO questions may now become your reading questions, so everyone must finish them.

In fact, the most important content to record is the viewpoint and logic of the passage. Keywords and terms are not important. Finish all listening TPOs before the exam, and there should be no major problem. I’ll share a few listening notes. Some students can get high scores without taking notes, so notes are not mandatory. It depends on the person.

### Speaking

Prepare for speaking during the month before the exam. If your speaking is really not fluent, you can consider taking a speaking class and practicing more. All I can say is that source materials are genuinely useful. For exams over a two-month period, independent speaking questions may hit similar source materials. A class also gives you a clearer understanding of each question type, and provides the structures and templates you need to pay attention to when answering. It is quite useful.

When actually reviewing, speaking can only be improved by speaking more yourself. You also need to be thick-skinned when speaking, because confidence is extremely important during the exam. Around you, there may be people who speak worse than you, and there may also be people who speak as well as native speakers. At that point, if your mindset becomes weak, problems will appear in your answers: the more you speak, the less confident you become; the more you speak, the quieter your voice becomes; the more you speak, the more monotonous your language becomes.

Independent speaking is about accumulating source materials. I prepared multiple types of source materials for myself so that when facing topics such as communication, culture, technology, and environmental protection, I would have something to say. At the same time, accumulate logical connectors: prepare two or three words for each type of relationship, such as cause and effect, progression, and contrast. Through structure and source materials, jointly build your own one-minute verbal bombardment. During the exam, make sure you can completely express a full viewpoint, including stance, reason, example, counterargument, and conclusion.

For integrated speaking, the more important part is actually listening: being able to write down all the test points. So this question type is relatively simple. The structure of the questions is also relatively stable, and it is a bit easier than listening questions. In the end, you only need to use templates or logical connectors you prepared to state the important test points.

For pronunciation practice, I recommend a free course here: [Lisa American English Phonetics Pronunciation Tutorial with Chinese Subtitles (4.0 Advanced Techniques)](https://www.bilibili.com/video/av33089221)


### Writing

For writing, I am not sure whether I should recommend that everyone prepare templates, because the first time I used a template, I scored 21; the second time I did not use a template, I scored 24. Of course, this may also be because I had some dedicated training in integrated writing listening, so perhaps my integrated writing score improved.

With a template, writing becomes relatively simple, and you will have some help with word count and advanced vocabulary.

Without a template, it is also not impossible, because the most important thing in the exam is whether your overall logic and the arguments you provide are relevant to the prompt and consistent with your stance.

Another point is typing speed. I can give everyone a reference: with 380 words for independent writing and 220 words for integrated writing, you can still get 24. Some experts start at 600 words, and the most extreme can reach 700 words. (How fast must their hands be?) I really admire them. It makes sense that they get 30.

Regarding spelling, I feel it has a major impact on the score. Before the exam, you should aim for no more than five spelling errors. In regular practice, do not use software with spell-check to practice writing! Prepare source materials together with speaking. In the two weeks before the exam, do one TPO set every two days, and make sure your writing score can stay stable at 4. Then there should be no major problem. Finally, first-time test-takers should prepare themselves mentally, because the real exam is different from mock tests. Listening may sometimes include British English, and the first speaking question may give you a sense that it suddenly starts out of nowhere.


Finally, after reviewing all the way to the end of this year, many students around me said they could not hold on anymore. Yes, this is indeed the hardest period in the process of preparing for the TOEFL. It is like being in a narrow tunnel, with only a faint glimmer of light ahead. Behind you, everything is pitch black, and there has long been no way back. Giving up is impossible. But when you want to fight, you also wonder whether that tiny glimmer of light is worth it. You feel as if everyone else is running under the sun, with victory fully assured, while your struggle becomes insignificant. Then you begin to hesitate. You still study every day, but you no longer have even the slightest vision of a better future. You only think: let this road end sooner. “Let me out” becomes your only expectation. But you have forgotten that nobody in this world has it easy. There is no such thing as running under the sun. Everyone is moving forward painfully through this same tunnel. One day in the future, when those so-called successful people look back on the road they traveled, they will certainly mention that back then, they too struggled painfully in a tunnel. Only then do you suddenly remember: I have been to that place too. When the road is narrow, the brave prevail. Do not belittle yourself. When future success is projected onto the present, it is only a glimmer of light. Everyone is the same. There is no bright sunshine, only a little faint light. But your effort has always been radiant. You forgot that all along this journey, it was not the future that gave you hope; it was you who kept giving yourself strength. What you need to embrace is not some bullshit success. Success is a bastard; it only attaches itself to powerful people. What you need to meet is a better version of yourself: the stronger self who refuses to admit defeat and refuses to give up. In fact, that little bit of light you saw was also given to you by that self. Do not disappoint the self waiting for you at the end of the tunnel, because if you give up, she will not be able to wait for you. And that petty villain called success will also leave her. Fight on! Burn bright! Go see, go meet, go embrace. Then one day, you will tell the story together with success: you followed a beam of light through the tunnel and found her. And one day, you will be able to smile as you talk about the moments that once made you cry. 💪
