# A First-Hand Account of Applying to CS Master’s Programs in the U.S. in the Post-Pandemic Era

<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_0.jpeg'></img></p>


The 21fall application cycle was unusually different. After the pandemic broke out worldwide in January 2020, campuses were closed, and international students were stranded in China taking online classes. On top of that came U.S.-China tensions, Presidential Proclamation 10043, the mutual closure of consulates general, the suspension of all nonimmigrant visa processing, and additional travel-ban NIE (National Interest Exceptions) restrictions, all of which made it even harder for Chinese students to study in the U.S. Although China quickly brought the pandemic under control, the U.S. case curve remained persistently high, making Chinese parents whose children were preparing to study in the U.S. extremely concerned. In this special post-pandemic era, what difficulties arise in the application process? I went through the entire process myself, and in this article I’ll discuss it in detail.

I explained the purpose and origin of my decision to study abroad in my 2019 year-end review. Everyone’s situation is different, and the decision is full of subjective factors, so I won’t discuss it in this article. Things related to preparing for the TOEFL and GRE were covered in my 2020 year-end review, so I won’t repeat them here either. This article assumes that you have already made up your mind to pursue a CS Master’s in the U.S. and have prepared your TOEFL & GRE scores. I’ll share what I saw, heard, and thought throughout the entire application process, along with the lessons and “takeaways” I gained. If you plan to apply this year, please calm down and read patiently to the end. The pitfalls encountered by someone who has been through it will definitely give you “plenty” to take away.

## 1. Why USA CS Master

<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_30.jpeg'></img></p>


Due to the restrictions of Presidential Proclamation 10043, students from the Seven Sons of National Defense and BUPT face visa limitations, so many outstanding students have shifted toward European schools. Europe has many excellent institutions. Beyond the famous Oxford and Cambridge, Switzerland and Germany also have top CS universities. For specific rankings, see [QS World University Rankings](https://www.topuniversities.com/university-rankings/world-university-rankings/2021).

> Regarding rankings, there are two authoritative rankings: [Qs](https://www.topuniversities.com/) and [USNews](https://www.usnews.com/). In the Qs ranking, UK universities tend to rank higher; in the USNews ranking, U.S. universities tend to rank higher.

In terms of rankings, UK and U.S. universities are neck and neck in the TOP10. But if you also consider the post-graduation job market, the U.S. still has the edge in CS, because of New York and Silicon Valley. Returning to school selection: students from the Seven Sons of National Defense and BUPT can consider Europe, Canada, Japan, Hong Kong, and Singapore. I have a basic understanding of universities around the world and summarized the following table:


||Obvious advantages|Obvious disadvantages|
|:-----:|:-----:|:-----:|
|U.S.|GPA does not distinguish between 985/211/non-985-or-211 backgrounds|Highest cost|
|UK|Programs are usually only one year, with lower cost|GPA evaluation distinguishes between 985/211/non-985-or-211 backgrounds and values undergraduate pedigree|
|Canada||Few CS TOP 100 schools; UoT stands alone|
|Japan||In addition to TOEFL/IELTS, you also need N1|
|Europe||In addition to TOEFL/IELTS, you also need exams for less commonly taught languages|
|Hong Kong||GPA evaluation distinguishes between 985/211/non-985-or-211 backgrounds and values undergraduate pedigree|
|Singapore||Average CS job market|
|Australia||Average CS job market|

If any of the obvious disadvantages above hits your weak point, I suggest you think carefully. For example, if you do not have a strong talent for languages, studying in Japan or Europe means dealing with two foreign-language exams, which is very exhausting; if your funding is not sufficient, consider the U.S. cautiously, because the U.S. is the most expensive; if you come from a non-985-or-211 institution, consider the UK and Hong Kong cautiously.

Take me as an example: I am from a non-985 university, so I was cautious about the UK and Hong Kong, or simply did not consider them. When filling out UK online application systems, GPA requirements differ: if you are from a prestigious 985 university, a GPA of 80 may be OK; if you are from a 211 university, a GPA of 85 may be OK; if you are from a non-985-or-211 university, even a GPA of 95 may not be OK. This is because applicants from non-prestigious schools are put into a separate pool. For non-prestigious-school students, there are very few chances to turn things around in the UK, except for those who are exceptionally outstanding. My language talent is average, and I spent a lot of time just learning English, so I directly ruled out Japan and Europe. I also care a lot about the CS job market, so I ruled out Australia and Singapore as well. After eliminating those options, the final choices should be the U.S. and Canada. The U.S. job market is better than Canada’s, so I leaned more toward the U.S.

> In the end, however, I still applied to one UK university, one Singaporean university, one Canadian university, and 14 U.S. universities. You might be curious: why did I stubbornly apply upward knowing I likely could not get in? I discovered the above patterns only after experiencing the process myself. If time could be reversed, I definitely would not have charged ahead so stubbornly.

CS Master’s programs in the UK/U.S./Singapore/Australia/Canada can also be divided into two types: Courser-base and Research-based. Courser-base is more employment-oriented (but not absolutely), while Research-based is more PhD-oriented (but also not absolutely). If readers are interested in Research, I suggest applying directly for PhD programs. To be safe, apply to MS/PhD Research programs together. Research-based programs are all called Master of Science in Computer Science with thesis; this is the only case, namely those beginning with MS.

All of the programs I applied to were Courser-base. Within Courser-base, they can be further divided into Terminal and non-Terminal programs. Terminal means “ending”: this program 100% cannot lead to a PhD, or in other words, completing this program provides no help when applying for a PhD. Examples include: Master of Computer Science, Master of Engineering, and Masters Program in Computer Science. More specifically: MSC@**UCI**, MSC@**RICE**, MPCS@**UChicago**, Meng EECS@**UCB**, Meng CS@**Cornell Tech**, MSE@**CMU SCS**. Non-Terminal programs are generally Master of Science in Computer Science without thesis, i.e., programs whose abbreviation starts with MS, translated into Chinese as “Master of Science.” These programs can lead to a PhD, or allow you to apply to the school’s PhD program after the first year.

If your goal is job hunting, you also need to pay attention to whether the program includes an internship. If there is an internship period, the school will give you CPT during the summer, and you can go do an internship. Some programs have no internship, and you may have classes during vacations, such as MSE@**CMU SCS**, which has classes during the break and does not provide CPT. You can still find a job in the U.S. from a program without CPT; you just need to find a job within 90 days after graduation to start using OPT. A program with CPT simply gives you one more chance to land a job, because during a CPT internship, if you perform well, you have a high probability of receiving a return offer. If you want to find a job and stay in the U.S., choosing a program with CPT internship opportunities is a must-have. At this point, you should have a clear sense of which country’s universities to choose, what type of master’s program to choose, whether to pursue a PhD, and whether you need an internship.


## 2. Application Materials

If you know me, you also know my background. If you don’t know my background, then there’s no need to know. My background was quite poor: non-985-or-211 undergraduate institution, TOEFL 98+, GRE 320+3, GPA 3.82/4.0, Major 3.9/4.0. My three-dimensional scores were not outstanding, probably lower-middle?

### 1. Undergraduate Background

If your undergraduate background is very strong, for example, from Tsinghua or Peking University, or from Nanjing University, Shanghai Jiao Tong University, Zhejiang University, or Fudan University, then congratulations: you can apply to most universities on Earth with your eyes closed, as freely as you like.

<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_27.jpg'></img></p>

If your school appears in the image above, you can apply to both the UK and the U.S. If your undergraduate school is not in the image above, I suggest not considering the UK, because your pedigree is not good enough. The image above is the ranking of mainland Chinese undergraduate institutions. If you come from an overseas undergraduate institution, you are naturally one tier above mainland undergraduates. For example, UCR is not as good as Tsinghua or Peking University, but when applying to U.S. master’s programs, it is more favored by admissions officers. First, because the undergraduate education was conducted entirely in English; second, because the priority of the overseas-undergraduate pool is higher than that of the mainland-undergraduate pool. For a UCR undergraduate applying to UCLA or UCB, it is easier than for a Tsinghua/Peking undergraduate applying to UCLA or UCB. Another example: when Stanford, MIT, and Harvard recruit from mainland Chinese undergraduates, they only recruit from Tsinghua/Peking + the “East China Five,” and sometimes not even all of the East China Five. But Stanford, MIT, and Harvard admit many overseas-undergraduate students, even if those schools rank below Tsinghua and Peking overall:

**Overseas undergrad > Tsinghua/Peking > NJU + ZJU + SJTU >> other East China Five + some CS-specialized schools（such as BUPT, HUST）> 211 > other non-985-or-211 schools**

If you are still in college, you can also improve your undergraduate background. Consider Transfer: study in China during freshman and sophomore years, then Transfer to a TOP50 U.S. undergraduate institution for junior and senior years. Graduating with a U.S. undergraduate degree improves your application to Master’s programs by more than one tier, and the cost-effectiveness is very high. Studying in the U.S. for junior and senior years costs around 1 million RMB. This path requires a total of 2 years to obtain a TOP20 Master’s degree, costing around 2 million RMB.

If you have already graduated, and your family has a lot of money to support your studies, you can consider attending a U.S. community college. This is equivalent to doing another undergraduate degree from scratch. U.S. community-college students can also transfer in junior year, transferring into a TOP50 U.S. university. People who take this route usually choose a community college in California, then transfer to one of the University of California schools. After graduating in senior year, they have effectively reached elite status. This path costs a great deal of time and money. Redoing an undergraduate degree takes 4 years, and 4 years in the U.S. costs at least around 2 million RMB. The 2 million is only the beginning: that only gets you a bachelor’s degree. Applying for and completing a Master’s for another 1–2 years will likely require another 1–1.5 million. This path requires a total of 6 years to obtain a TOP20 Master’s degree, costing around 3–3.5 million RMB.

### 2. GPA


<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_31.jpg'></img></p>

As an important part of the three-dimensional scores, the importance of GPA needs no further explanation. **Some schools pay particular attention to GPA**, such as USC and UoT. They rank applicants by GPA from high to low, then issue offers from top to bottom by priority. If you are still a freshman, sophomore, or junior, please plan your time carefully and aim to make your GPA as high as possible. If your undergraduate school allows GPA improvement, you can consider finishing all courses by junior year, then retaking freshman and sophomore courses with low GPAs in senior year to boost your GPA.

Some schools require official transcript verification via CHSI, while others require original transcripts to be mailed during the online application stage. Please prepare these materials in advance. Use the degree-verification website to authenticate your transcript, and get a translated copy as well. After completing verification, the specific process is to pay for an additional English version. Schools that require CHSI-authenticated transcripts, such as WUSTL, will ask you to send undergraduate materials through CHSI: authenticated degree certificate (Chinese and English) + authenticated transcript (Chinese and English). Schools that require original transcripts to be mailed, such as UMich and NYU, require you to contact your undergraduate school, have the relevant teacher stamp them with the academic affairs office seal, seal them in the school’s envelope, stamp again over the seal, and mail them from the school. Multiple stamps + mailing from the school are intended to ensure the envelope was not opened and altered in transit.

There is another thing you must know about GPA. In the U.S., there is an educational organization called WES, which specializes in GPA evaluation. After its evaluation, your GPA will very likely be higher than the GPA on your school transcript. This is because they do not recognize domestic politics-related courses, such as “Principles of Marxist Philosophy,” “Outline of Modern and Contemporary Chinese History,” “Ideological and Moral Cultivation and Fundamentals of Law,” and “Introduction to Mao Zedong Thought and the Theoretical System of Socialism with Chinese Characteristics,” nor domestic PE and health courses. When recalculating GPA, they do not include them. The resulting GPA will be somewhat higher than the one on the university transcript. Therefore, for schools that allow WES-evaluated transcripts, submit WES evaluations whenever possible; this indirectly raises your GPA. Although fewer and fewer schools support WES-evaluated transcripts, some still do. For example, several CMU programs still support WES evaluation. USC and NYU do not support it; they only accept the original GPA on your university transcript.

### 3. TOEFL & GRE

<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_32.jpg'></img></p>

There is no need to say much about language scores: they are the most important part of the three-dimensional scores. Whether you study by yourself or take a course, please obtain your target score as soon as possible. It is really not worth dragging out the entire application process because your GT scores are delayed. My English is not great either, and there are no shortcuts in learning. Everyone’s method is different. Most readers seeing this article may already have their language scores, so this article will not spend space focusing on language-learning experience. Interested readers can look through my 2019 and 2020 year-end reviews.

### 4. Soft Background

<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_28.png'></img></p>

Soft background includes many things: summer research, top-conference journal papers, academic competitions (ACM, CTF, MCM/ICM, etc.), exchange-student experience, multiple FLAAGTM internships, and other global awards (Apple Scholarship, etc.).

> FLAAGTM = Facebook, LinkedIn, Amazon, Apple, Google, Twitter, Microsoft
> 

Improving soft background is a matter of “the Eight Immortals crossing the sea, each showing their own powers.” Strong applicants have top-tier three-dimensional scores and fully stacked soft backgrounds: multiple summer research experiences at top universities such as MIT, Stanford, CMU, and UCB; several top-conference papers, the more the better; gold medals in academic competitions; exchange-student experiences at top universities such as Stanford, MIT, and Harvard; and multiple FLAAGTM internships. Because everyone’s energy and ability are limited, do your best to obtain the most valuable awards or honors in the world.

I want to say a bit more about summer research. Summer research is a “surprise weapon.” Although it is not a decisive factor, it often has the special effect of winning by surprise. In my view, the main purpose of summer research is not necessarily to produce research results, but more to showcase yourself to Professors at top schools. Among this group of summer research students, how to stand out and become exceptional may be more “critical.” If you are good enough, you can complete PhD outreach during the summer research period and try to obtain a Strong Recommend letter from the Professor. With this recommendation letter, when the fall online application system opens, apply to that school immediately; this recommendation-letter item can create a large gap between you and comparable competitors.

Finally, readers should note: if your three-dimensional scores are already “settled,” your GPA cannot be improved, and your GT scores have also hit a bottleneck, unable to break through 110+/330+, then please spend more time improving your soft background. Master’s applications do not look only at three-dimensional scores; admission is the result of evaluating overall strength. Since the hard background has already taken shape, work harder on the soft background~ My three-dimensional scores were basically “terrible,” but I relied on some passable work experience to “max out” my soft background, and in the end I was admitted by CMU.

### 5. CV, Essays, and Recommendation Letters

<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_29.jpeg'></img></p>

For the CV, just write truthfully. Highlight your strengths across multiple dimensions: education, exchange-student experience, internships, summer research, top-conference papers, competition awards, and other soft background.

The PS essay needs to be written according to each school’s requirements. Different schools have different programs, and within different programs there are also different tracks. How to align your own characteristics with the program’s requirements is the most important consideration when writing essays. Essays generally include your highlight experiences, why school, and why program. The why school section tests most strongly whether the student truly understands the school. This involves many aspects, such as whether you understand the campus culture, whether you understand the research directions of various faculty members, whether you know which labs and research groups you are interested in, whether you understand the school’s local reputation and social value, and so on. Some of this deeper content is on the school’s homepage, some is in the school’s introduction videos, and some is in campus interviews. If you carefully watch and collect information, you can find part of it yourself, but this part is extremely, extremely, extremely time-consuming. I spent a particularly large amount of time understanding each school, each program, and each faculty member’s research preferences within each school. Altogether, it took about 2 weeks.


Some programs require a Video Essay / Video Interview in addition to the PS, as well as a Diversity Essay. These sections are equally important. Recording a Video Essay for the first time can be confusing; you may not know what content to record. Most of your highlights have already been reflected in the CV and PS, and the Essay does not allow repetition. The Diversity Essay is also painful. Diversity is a unique gene of American culture. You might say your academic ability is strong and you have published more than ten top-conference papers, which is unique; or your GPA/TOEFL/GRE scores are very high, which is unique. None of these are Diversity. The Diversity Essay likewise cannot repeat content from the CV and PS, and it requires 1000 words. This part needs to be written according to the school’s “DNA.” If a school serves society, then whether your past experience includes community-service-related content matters; if so, you can write about it. If a school leads local technology, then whether your past includes cases that demonstrate your leadership matters; if so, you can write about them. Overall, the Diversity Essay is still fairly difficult to write.

Finally, recommendation letters. The basic requirement is 3 letters. A more appropriate combination is 2 academic professors and 1 internship leader. Try to get strong recommendation letters for all 3. If you have summer research experience, try to ask a professor from that school to write your recommendation letter; when applying to that school, this letter will have an advantage. For academic professors, try to choose faculty whose academic papers are highly influential. If there is a professor at your school who has co-authored research papers with professors at the school you want to apply to, prioritize that professor to write your recommendation letter. The same logic applies to recommendation letters from internship/work mentors/leaders: prioritize internationally well-known people; if none are available, try to choose someone from a well-known major domestic company.

**A strong recommendation from a professor at the school you are applying to > a strong recommendation from a professor who has collaborated with admissions officers or professors at the school you are applying to > a well-known professor in overseas academia > a well-known leader in overseas industry > a well-known professor in domestic academia > a well-known leader in domestic industry > others**

Recommendation letters are an “advantage” for mainland-undergraduate students. If you communicate in advance with professors you have a good relationship with, the letters they provide are usually strong recommendations. For overseas-undergraduate students, there are pitfalls in this part! Many overseas professors may verbally say they strongly recommend you, but some of that is polite phrasing; the final recommendation letter may be lukewarm or even negative. Many overseas professors are quite straightforward and write whatever they think. If they expose too many of your shortcomings, it may become a negative recommendation. Of course, most professors are very nice; I just want everyone to be careful and guard against negative recommendations.

There is one more thing mainland-undergraduate students need to pay attention to: “anti-cheating.” I saw this case on 1Point3Acres. This year, many schools added anti-fraud monitoring for recommendation letters. For example, an applicant handles all 3 recommenders and writes the recommendation letters for them. The recommendation-letter webpage records the IP address, the local path of the uploaded file, even the hostname, and the submission time. Based on these 3 items, it can determine whether the recommendation letters were uploaded from the same machine. If all 3 recommendation letters were uploaded from the same machine, and even at the same moment or within a very short time, then it can be concluded that the applicant handled all recommendation letters themselves. Under normal circumstances, this would be impossible. The normal process should be that the 3 machines have different hostnames, the upload times are irregularly spaced, and the IP addresses are different. In addition to “anti-cheating” checks on upload time and upload machine, pdf and Word software can also be checked. For example, the applicant single-handedly handles the recommendation letters of 3 recommenders on their own computer, writing them in Word or pdf on their own computer. On a Mac or Windows computer, using Microsoft Office Word 365 requires activation. Once activated and logged into an email account, an edited Word document will write “source” and “author” into the file information. If the school checks the file information of the uploaded Word or pdf files and finds that all three recommendation letters come from the same person, it can basically conclude that they were written by the same person. Under normal circumstances, the “author” fields in the Word and pdf file information generated on three different computers should be different.

I applied to UoT this year. During the interview stage, there were questions specifically about recommendation letters. Some students were asked by admissions officers: “From the system, it appears that all 3 of your recommendation letters were uploaded from the same computer. Please explain why.” Obviously, the admissions officer suspected the authenticity of the recommendation letters and suspected that the student may have handled them all personally. Being asked such questions during the interview is extremely stressful, and the atmosphere is also extremely awkward. According to the student’s own account on 1Point3Acres, he answered: “Because the professor was very busy, I carried my laptop to his office, found him, and urged him to complete it on my computer.” This answer does not look very good. I am sharing this real case I saw with everyone. After reading it, readers must keep in mind the authenticity of application materials. As for what exactly you should do, you should understand by now.

## III. Application Process

### 1. Selecting Schools and Programs

<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_33.jpeg'></img></p>


For CS programs at U.S. TOP50 schools, the typical thresholds among the “three metrics” are roughly TOEFL around 100, GRE around 320–325, and GPA 3.5+. These three metrics determine the tier of schools you can target; the higher, the better. My own scores were fairly average, but I also had dream schools, so I applied across a very wide range, from TOP 1 to TOP 70. I divided them into four categories: lottery schools (TOP 10), reach schools (TOP 10–20), target schools (around TOP 30), and safety schools (TOP 40–70). Someone on the forum ranked the application difficulty of U.S. CS Master’s programs as follows:


||Program|
|:-----:|:-------:|
|tier 0|MSCS@**Standford**, MSCS@**MIT**, MSCS@**CMU**, MSCS@**UCB**, MSCS@**Princeton**|
|tier 1|Meng@**UCB**, MSCS@**UCLA**, MSCS@**UT Austin**, Meng@**Cornell Tech**, MSCS@**Wisconsin Madison**, MSCS@**Harvard**|
|tier 1.5|MSCS@**UIUC**, MSCS@**GaTech**, MSCS@**UPenn**, MSCS@**UM Ann Arbor**|
|tier 2|MSCS@**Columbia**, MSCS@**UCSD**, MSCSE(COC)@**GaTech**, MSCS@**Brown**, MSCS@**UMich**, MSCS@**Duke**, MSCS@**Dartmouth**, MSCS@**Yale**, MSCS@**Purdue**, MSCS@**Washington**|
|tier 3| MCS@**Rice**, MSCS28@**USC**, MSCS@**NYU**, MSCS@**NWU**, MSCS@**UCD**, MCS@**UCI**, MSCS@**JHU**, MPCS@**UChicago**, MSCS@**Virginia**, MSCS@**UCSB**, MSCS@**Stony Brook**, MSCS@**Virginia Tech**|

tier 0 is the hardest to get into. The four strongest CS schools in North America—CMU, MIT, Stanford, and UC. Berkeley—are truly top-tier, and their MSCS programs are extremely difficult to apply to successfully. Why is Princeton in tier 0 together with the big four? Because this program has very high speaking requirements; applicants with speaking scores below 25 are basically rejected. It also has very high expectations for academic background. According to statistics from the forum, in the past two years very few applicants from mainland Chinese undergraduate programs have been admitted.

tier 0–3 covers the TOP40 U.S. CS Master’s programs. Judging from my own admission results, my level was only enough for tier 3 and some safety schools. The three dream lottery tickets I bought this year were MSCS@**CMU**, MCDS@**CMU**, and MSCS@**Gatech**; all rejected me. Climbing this ladder is indeed not easy, and every tier is hard to move up. You can try to climb upward according to your own strength.

For students from EE, ECE, or liberal arts backgrounds who want to switch into CS, you must pay special attention to programs that are friendly to career/major switchers, such as MPCS@**UChicago**, CS Align@**NEU**, and CS37@**USC**. These programs are specifically designed for students switching majors.


On the question of getting two CS Master’s degrees: if you already have a CS Master’s and apply for another CS Master’s, you will run into one issue—the admissions committee will need you to explain why you want another Master’s degree. So far, I also have not seen any big names on LinkedIn who hold two CS Master’s degrees. Let me irresponsibly speculate a little about how admissions officers may think: they may prefer to give the opportunity to applicants who do not already have a CS Master’s.

As for getting multiple Master’s degrees in general, that is outside the scope of this article. I can briefly mention it. Usually, after completing a CS Master’s, you get three years of OPT and enter the H1B lottery. What if you do not get selected? And you still want to stay in the U.S.? Then you continue with another Master’s or apply for a PhD. If you cannot get into a PhD program, you continue with another Master’s. Usually you can choose something related to your field; for example, CS students can choose DS. You can also choose something unrelated, such as another degree in Music or Laws. There is also a class of Master’s programs that are almost like paying for CPT. These Master’s programs allow you to use CPT immediately upon enrollment, commonly known as “day 1 CPT,” meaning you can start an internship as soon as you enroll. During the internship, you can enter the H1B lottery. You only need to go to campus a few times per week. In any case, if you have money, there are quite a few ways to hang on in the U.S. That is enough digression; let’s get back to the main topic.

### 2. Online Applications

After filling out the online application materials, be sure to carefully check every section to avoid low-level mistakes. Some schools do not put their PS requirements on the program homepage; instead, they are in the online application system. So register your account as early as possible, read their requirements carefully (for example, font size, line spacing, word count, etc.), and plan your time reasonably.

This stage is quite critical. You must plan your deadlines and submission strategy well. Different schools have different application windows: some are rolling, and some have two rounds. The online application submission strategy has a major impact on the final result! Schools generally admit more students in the first round, so for the programs you most want to attend or are very confident about, you should submit in the first round. Add a few safety schools to the first round as well. I suffered a loss in the 21fall application cycle. In the first round of selection for NEU’s MSCS, someone with GPA 3.5/TOEFL 95/GRE 315 was admitted. My three metrics were higher than that, so logically I should have been quite safe, but I was not admitted; instead, I was advised to switch to COE, and after switching I was indeed admitted. Also, MSCS@**NEU** was very strange at the time: on the same day my status changed to under review, I was rejected. For a while, I suspected the admissions officer had not even read my materials and had simply rejected me directly. Later, after multiple confirmations on the forum, this kind of same-day review and instant rejection was very likely because that round of rolling admissions had already filled up. I submitted MSCS@**NEU** on February 3; the Recommend Letter was Complete on February 10; the status changed to under review on February 13; and I was rejected the same day. NEU’s first-round submission period was November 2020. My experience tells you that if you want to apply to NEU MSCS, I recommend submitting it as soon as the online application opens. Even if it is a safety school, secure it first. This shows that submission strategy affects the round you are considered in, which in turn affects whether you can get an offer.


In the second round, you can apply to some lottery schools where you are less confident and a small number of target schools. Of course, if you finish improving your language scores before September, spend September fully focused on writing each school’s essays, and submit all schools in the first round in October, that is also a very good strategy.

> Also, before submitting online applications, please pay close attention to application fee waivers. Some schools let you apply to one program and get another program free, i.e., they waive the application fee for one program. NEU and USC both have waiver options. Some programs across certain NEU colleges can waive application fees between each other. For the exact situation, please refer to that year’s admissions instructions. Among USC’s multiple CS programs, at most one program’s application fee can be waived. I was rather foolish: although I knew a waiver was available, I paid for all three programs when making payment. The correct operation should have been to submit one program first, then contact the school to get a discount code, so that when submitting the second program, the discount code could waive the application fee. Then submit the third program and pay for it. Applying to three USC programs actually only requires paying two application fees. I hope future readers can learn from my mistake here and save one application fee.


### 3. Interviews

Many programs have an interview component. This means that during the application season, applicants not only need to rush their PS essays, but also prepare for interviews at the same time. Several of the programs I applied to this year had interviews: MEng CS@**Cornell**, MSCS@**WUSTL**, MSE@**CMU**, MITS@**CMU**, MSCS@**Columbia**, and MScAC@**UoT**. Interviews come in several types: Skype voice interviews, scheduled Kira interviews, face-to-face Zoom interviews, and Coding Tests. A Coding Test is similar to LeetCode: 4 questions in 1 hour. You write the code and test it yourself, but there is no OJ to judge correctness, and after submitting the code you cannot modify it. The final score is jointly determined by completion time and the number of test case groups passed. Voice or video interview questions vary widely. Simple questions may ask why school, testing how much you know about the school and how strongly you like it. Harder questions may ask about work/internship experience, summer research experience, exchange student experience, and research experience based on your CV. Even harder questions may ask technical topics, such as which design patterns you know and what scenarios each design pattern applies to. They may ask whether you took algorithms courses as an undergraduate and which algorithms you learned. They may ask whether you studied linear algebra and probability theory; if so, explain concepts such as matrix rank, inverse matrices, change of basis, etc., what Bayes’ theorem is, mathematical expectation, the Poisson distribution, Markov chains, and so on. In data structures, they may ask how red-black trees rotate and adjust nodes... When asked technical or mathematical questions, I think the hardest part is how to express the answer in English and make sure the interviewer understands. I was asked what the Bridge pattern is, what its definition is, and where the Bridge pattern is used. I could explain it perfectly in Chinese, but switching to English made it very “bumpy.”

This has to do with not reading enough technical books in pure English on a regular basis. If you routinely read technical articles in English, you will naturally pick up the technical terms through exposure. When answering interview questions, if you encounter something you do not know how to describe, you can describe it in another way. But this method is less professional than using one precise word. It is possible that you say a lot, and then the interviewer prompts you with the one key word, and you suddenly realize, “Yes, exactly, that is what I meant.” For students who want to stand out in technical interviews, you should know what to do, right? Read a lot of technical articles in English in your daily routine.


### 4. Choosing an Offer

Offers are generally released after Christmas in the U.S., meaning after mid-to-late January of the following year. The wave of offers in late January corresponds to applications submitted between November 15 and November 30. Large batches of offers are concentrated in late February and early March. After receiving an offer, first confirm the reply deadline; you must make a decision before that date, otherwise the offer expires. After accepting an offer, some schools also require a deposit to hold your place. Some schools have very high deposits, such as NEU $750, Columbia $4800, and NYU $500. The deposit raises the threshold for applicants to renege and jump to another school. If you accept an offer and later go back on it, that is very irresponsible behavior toward the school. To make applicants decide carefully, schools add high deposits, which also increases their own revenue. For example, suppose you accept Columbia’s offer, but one month after paying the deposit you receive an offer from Stanford, so you jump ship to Stanford. By raising the deposit threshold, even if you leave, the school still earns your $4800 for free. Of course, during the pandemic, some schools canceled deposits because they wanted to attract more students to enroll. I hope both sides can be kind to each other: applicants should not take advantage of schools that do not charge deposits by casually breaking commitments, and schools should not act like players by withholding results forever and making applicants wait indefinitely.

Generally, offer deadlines range from two weeks to two months. After receiving an offer, learn as much as possible as soon as possible about the pitfalls of the program and decide whether it is the best fit for you. Dynamically identify the program that suits you best among multiple offers. Anxiety from overthinking every possible trade-off can destroy your mindset. Please remember: there is no perfect program in the world, only the program that best fits you. If it is not an offer from MSCS@**Stanford**, basically every other program will involve trade-offs. You need to consider factors such as school ranking, geographic location, weather and climate, curriculum, employment data, alumni network, professors’ research directions, and so on.


### 5. Preparing I-20 Materials

After deciding which school’s offer to ultimately accept, you need to start preparing I-20 materials. These materials include verifying personal identity information, financial proof, status transfer for U.S. undergraduate students, and so on. For students from mainland Chinese undergraduate programs, the main item is financial proof. The school will give you the program’s estimated cost, and you need to obtain a bank-issued proof of funds greater than that estimate. I-20 materials are very important; only after receiving the I-20 can you start applying for a U.S. visa. The U.S. visa interview application requires you to fill in the I-20 Number. Different schools process at different speeds, so submit the materials as soon as possible so that you can receive the I-20 earlier.

For my 21fall case, I accepted CMU’s offer, and after submitting materials, I still needed to wait 4–6 weeks for I-20 processing. At other schools—UIUC, NYU, Rice, WUSTL, Tufts, UTD—after applicants submitted I-20 materials, the I-20 could be issued within one week. So please be sure to prepare the proof of funds as early as possible and submit the required I-20 application materials early. My suggestion is that after all online applications are completed, you can start preparing your proof of funds. For a typical 16-month program, preparing 700k–900k RMB is definitely enough. This way, once an offer arrives and you decide which school to attend, you can submit the proof of funds that same day and join the I-20 queue. I only started preparing the I-20 after receiving the offer, which took another week. In the end, classmates who submitted materials one week earlier than I did received their I-20 10 days earlier than me. I waited more than 7 weeks, close to 2 months. In the middle, the school also had a holiday for its anniversary. So preparing the financial proof materials for I-20 as early as possible is definitely the right choice. Once you get the I-20 earlier, you have much more control over scheduling the visa interview.

Regarding the funding source for financial proof, writing self-funding is truly a “big trap.” If I had the chance to do it again, I would definitely not write self-funding; I would write parental sponsorship. At first, I thought it did not matter what I wrote, so I transferred all the money to a card under my own name and froze all the funds. After submitting the I-20, the school reviewed it and rejected it, with the reason: “self-funding requires full financial proof; additional materials are needed.” The I-20 application materials stated that you only needed to submit financial proof covering all costs for the first year (tuition + living expenses + rent and all other expenses). But if it is self-funding, you need to provide full proof covering all costs for two years (tuition + living expenses + rent and all other expenses). This rule was not written on the school website; it was a hidden rule! If it had been written, I would not have chosen self-funding! The consequences of choosing self-funding were:

1. I needed to prepare additional proof for the second year and submit it again as supplementary material.
2. After submitting the supplementary material, I had to wait another week for the school to review it.

Both of these are quite “deadly.” For the first point, if you do not have a lot of savings, doubling the required financial proof may be hard to handle. For ordinary families, producing 1.5M RMB or more in financial proof all at once is very difficult. Even if they have that amount of money, it is unlikely to be sitting in a bank demand deposit account; it is usually spread across various investment, wealth management, fund, and stock accounts. (Of course, this excludes families worth billions who keep 1.5M RMB in cash at home as pocket money.) Withdrawing money from various scattered accounts takes several days back and forth.

For the second point, for a school like CMU that rigidly takes 6 weeks to review I-20 materials, supplementing materials once means it will take even longer for your I-20 materials to be approved. After resubmitting materials, the school still needs one week to review your supplementary materials. In the end, it takes 7 and a half weeks for the materials to be approved, close to 2 months or 8 weeks. For all the reasons above, if you insist on choosing self-funding, please be sure to prepare sufficiently large proof of deposits before your first submission, and do not go back and forth in the middle!

For fresh college graduates, the normal choice should be parental sponsorship. If you write self-funding instead, you may even be rejected during the F-1 visa interview. The visa officer OV may ask you, “You just graduated—where did this 1.5M RMB in cash come from?” Maybe you can explain it well (“I won the lottery,” “This is my first bucket of gold from starting a business in college,” “I earned it by trading stocks, funds, and cryptocurrency”), but if you interview in Singapore, you will be rejected directly; if you interview in China, your funding source may be checked. So fresh college graduates should not hesitate: be sure to write parental sponsorship. As for applicants who are already working, either option is fine, but note that once you write self-funding, you need to provide financial proof for the full cost of the entire program.

## IV. Changes in the Post-Pandemic Era

After the outbreak in Wuhan in January 2020, all offline TOEFL / GRE test centers across China were closed. They did not reopen until July 2020. I only managed to grab a rare test slot in an extremely remote area in August; it was truly difficult. In September and October, I traveled back and forth to remote Chinese cities to take offline TOEFL and GRE exams. Fortunately, the battle “ended” in November. In fact, my final scores were not that high. I had originally planned to take the exams four more times to improve my scores. Because of various DDLs, a lot of time was compressed; I took two more exams, but the scores were still not high, so I canceled the last two attempts. I recommend that readers who have gotten this far finish both the TOEFL and GRE before September, with scores of 105+ and 325+. My language-test scores took far too long to come out. I can’t really blame the pandemic; I can only blame my poor English skills. If I had been strong enough, I should have gotten the scores I needed from the first offline exam in September. So starting from the online-application stage, let’s talk about what has changed in study-abroad applications in the post-pandemic era.


### 1. Applications Exploded, and Involution Went Through the Roof

**The improvement of the global pandemic situation was the main reason why study-abroad applications surged by 50% in 2021**: In 2021, China achieved comprehensive domestic control of the pandemic, with many days of zero new cases nationwide; daily new cases were basically imported from abroad. The safe, comfortable, and free environment at home created a temporary illusion of global safety for many students, which also rekindled many people’s dream of studying abroad.

**A sense of opportunism was the trigger behind the 50% surge in study-abroad applications in 2021**: International-student tuition is one of the main funding sources for major universities abroad. Therefore, in 2021, major universities around the world gradually relaxed admissions policies for international students in order to attract them. Well-known U.S. universities such as Harvard, MIT, and Stanford announced that they would no longer require standardized test scores such as SAT, ACT, and GRE (tests that used to be mandatory for admission). In addition, many people had the lucky mindset that application competition would be weaker in 2021 and that their chances of getting into world-class universities would increase. For applicants this year, **getting into the Ivy League would become even harder**. This led to a surge in applicants, forcing schools to delay admission results.

The 20fall cohort of international students did not go abroad because of the pandemic, and this year, for 21fall, they continued applying, increasing the applicant pool. Since 20fall students already had an offer in hand, they had no burden when applying again for 21fall: if they failed to get into a better school, they could continue with their current school; if they succeeded, they could enroll in the better school in 21fall. ### 2. Work-from-Home Efficiency Was “Not High”

Because of the pandemic, U.S. university campuses closed, and all universities became “Online Universities.” Classes were held on Zoom, and admissions officers also worked from home. The impact of working from home was low efficiency. If you urgently needed to contact the admissions office, for example to associate a physical transcript with the Application ID in the online application system, chances were high that no one would pick up when you called. That is because the call went to the school, while the staff were working from home. With no other choice, you would email the admissions office, but they would also reply very slowly. They would ignore you for five days in a week. So in the second week, you would keep emailing to follow up. In the third week, the staff would suddenly reply with a templated response: “Do not chase us! This is peak application season, and the admissions office is already working at full capacity.” At that point, I no longer had the energy to struggle and could only “wait in a Buddhist way.” A month later, it was finally linked. To be fair, if this level of efficiency were in China’s public-service system, people would definitely file complaints. But after all, this is the work style of the U.S.; you cannot really complain. They only work five days a week, definitely do not work overtime on weekends, and leave on time in the evening on workdays. I am not sure what their efficiency was like before the pandemic. (Sometimes I really wished there were a DING feature so I could make nonstop death-call reminders. Of course, that could only be a thought. If you annoy the admissions officer, whoever chases gets rejected. Admissions officers are the masters; you have to serve them.)


Complaints aside, the “not high” in the title is also in quotation marks. Thinking rationally about this phenomenon, there is also logic behind it. First, U.S. universities have a very strict review process for applicant materials. Admissions officers follow this process strictly, one step after another, with rigorous procedures. Add to that the fact that applications surged dramatically, plus the impact of working from home, and it is understandable that admission-result release dates were delayed by one month.


### 3. Rushing to Singapore for a U.S. Visa

> This article began in April 2021, when U.S. embassies and consulates in Mainland China had not yet reopened. By the time readers see this article, the Mainland China consulates may already be open. After some hesitation, I still decided to keep this section, because rushing to Singapore for a U.S. visa is an experience that the 20fall and 21fall cohorts will never forget.

Due to the pandemic, domestic consulates suspended U.S.-visa services. Coupled with U.S. NIE restrictions, travelers who had been in China within 14 days before entering the U.S. were prohibited from entry. Therefore, going to a third country to apply for a U.S. visa + “washing” travel history became a necessity. At least 100,000 international students from 20fall + 21fall needed to go to Singapore for visas. (This indirectly boosted Singapore’s tourism industry.)

#### Preparations for Scheduling a Visa Interview in Singapore

1. Transfer the CGI account to Singapore
Because the visa-appointment systems in Mainland China and Singapore are both CGI appointment systems, you cannot register twice.<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_22.png'></img></p>   
So if students have already scheduled an interview in China, they need to transfer their domestic CGI account to Singapore. They can contact the embassy by email or phone to transfer it. If they have not scheduled an interview before, they can register a new CGI appointment account in Singapore.
2. Fill out the DS-160B form  
3. Pay the U.S. visa fee in Singapore  
Pay the visa fee on the official U.S. visa website. The U.S. visa fee in Singapore does not support online credit-card payment; it must be paid with a Singapore bank card or locally in cash.<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_23.png'></img></p><p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_24.png'></img></p> 
If you have friends in Singapore, generate your own Personal CGI reference number, save the PDF, and send it to your Singapore friend to help pay. After payment succeeds, ask your friend to send you the payment receipt. If you do not have friends in Singapore, generate your own Personal CGI reference number, print this payment slip, and take it to any Singapore Post office to pay in cash. Keep the payment receipt.
4. Schedule the interview time  
On the second working day after successfully paying the visa fee, once the visa fee becomes effective, you can schedule an interview. After the appointment is successfully made, you will receive an Appointment Confirmation letter. Print it and bring it to the interview. Important: According to the U.S. consulate’s 14-day travel ban, you can only go to the U.S. consulate for visa processing after receiving a negative PCR test in Singapore and staying locally for 14 full days. If the day you land in Singapore is Day 0, the interview must be scheduled no earlier than Day 15. After the interview in Singapore, the passport will be returned to the applicant. Generally, within 3-5 working days after the interview, you will receive a pickup notification email from the visa center. You can choose the pickup method yourself: collect it in person or have it mailed. Finally, depending on your actual situation, choose whether to go directly from Singapore to the U.S. or return to China.

### 4. Policy Changes Are Unpredictable

Policy changes can easily disrupt a person’s original plans. Due to the pandemic and the China-U.S. relationship, policies around study-abroad visas were truly changing all the time. Let me recap the changes over the past few months.

<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_1.png'></img></p>

February was anxious waiting. February 11, 2021 was Chinese New Year’s Eve. A few days before that, I received a rejection from Cornell. During the Spring Festival, I also received rejections from NEU and CMU. Three consecutive rejections triggered a dropout warning. During that period, every morning when I woke up I refreshed my email and checked admission results on 1Point3Acres. After lunch at noon I refreshed my email, and before bed at night I refreshed my email. A new term was circulating online: “frantic refreshers,” referring to people who are burning with anxiety every day and frantically refreshing their email. That term described me that month perfectly.

March brought some excitement. CMU started releasing results one after another, and I received two CMU offers in a row. At that point my mindset became much calmer, and I started preparing to look for a new job. The plan was to work in China until the end of the year, earn some money, and then go to the U.S. campus for classes in 22spring. If the pandemic did not improve in the fall, I would consider taking online classes from China.

April was anxious waiting again. CMU suddenly announced that offers could no longer be deferred. If you could not arrive on campus on time in September, the offer would automatically become invalid. Once this policy came out, it touched a nerve for many applicants. I immediately submitted my I-20 materials and prepared to fly to Singapore at any time. Because of CMU’s policy, the nature of work over the next few months had to be remote. This also disrupted my plan to join ByteDance. Without this CMU policy, I would have 100% joined ByteDance. I would not get to eat at ByteDance’s luxurious domestic cafeteria, nor wear ByteDance’s viral employee badge and become one of the elite. That dream could only be realized at TikTok in the U.S.

May was continued anxious waiting. CMU’s I-20 materials were very slow and required a 4-6 week wait. Following that timeline, I would not receive it until June. In May, due to policy changes, the NIE exemption was lifted for Chinese students going to the U.S. Many people around me who had booked tickets to Singapore felt completely screwed. On May 4, U.S. consulates in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenyang in China began processing F-1 student visas. Once again, this taught this cohort of international students a lesson. Many had already decided to go to Singapore: Singapore tourist visa, air-travel exemption, Airbnb rental, SIM card, and flight tickets were all done, and then suddenly domestic visa appointments reopened. Quite a few students who had already been checked in Singapore were crying in the restroom. Even worse, some extremely impulsive kids had not yet received their I-20 but had already registered their U.S. visa CGI in Singapore, filled out the DS-160, and paid the fee. The consequence of blindly rushing to Singapore was losing this 1000 RMB. At this point, large numbers of international students began transferring their U.S. visa CGI accounts back to China. The related study-abroad visa groups and Singapore visa groups all exploded over those few days; if you did not check WeChat for just one short hour, 2,000 messages could accumulate. I was also one of the people who blindly rushed to Singapore, and I was forced to transfer my CGI back as well. I frantically called the embassy.

After two painful days of calling, I finally transferred CGI back to China mainland. Calls to Shanghai in China would automatically disconnect after timing out. After about 60 minutes with no answer, the call would automatically disconnect. The waiting prompt was friendly, with silence and a slight electric hum. Calls to Beijing would not disconnect; you could wait indefinitely until someone answered. The waiting prompt was friendly, with a normal phone ringing tone. The Singapore embassy would also not disconnect; you could “wait indefinitely” until someone answered. The waiting prompt was unfriendly: it was the sound of horse racing. I was already anxious while waiting, and pairing that with horse-racing sounds really messed with my head. I did not test whether Singapore could truly wait indefinitely. I waited 30 minutes and was automatically disconnected because my phone credit was used up and the line was suspended. Calls to Singapore are international long-distance calls. Once connected, billing starts. Even if no one answers, you are charged during the wait because you have connected to the system. I initially did not know the billing rule, so I paid to listen to horse-racing sounds for 30 minutes at one yuan per minute. It was really expensive. In the end, I got through to the U.S. embassy phone line and chose English. There was a lot of English broadcast in the middle, some of which you might not understand, but it does not matter; it is all about U.S. visa policies, so it is fine if you do not understand it very well. When prompted to press number keys, press 1-2-2-2-5 in sequence to reach a human agent (note that you should not press them all at once; enter them in five separate steps, only when the phone prompt asks you to input). After waiting 10-20 minutes, someone answered the phone. "Could please help me to transfer my CGI from Singapore to China mainland?", “Sure, Of course.” After that, they asked me to provide my passport number, name and spelling, birthday, and other verification information. The operation was completed in about 3 minutes.

### 5. First Attempt

My first visa attempt was rejected. I took two days off and flew from Shanghai to Anjialou in Beijing. I thought I might get lucky, but the outcome was exactly the opposite. The hotel I booked was very cheap and was right next to the U.S. Embassy in Beijing. After my flight landed the night before, I checked into the hotel in pouring rain.


<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_44.png'></img></p>

The U.S. Embassy in Beijing is truly huge, like an oversized siheyuan compound. This is what it looks like from the outside. Phones are not allowed inside, so I do not have any photos to show. June 17 was the day of my visa interview. It had rained heavily the previous day, but the next morning the sky cleared up. I thought that might be a good omen. After breakfast at the hotel, I took the elevator downstairs. The elevator was full of students holding folders. It was obvious they were there for visa interviews. I followed the crowd and arrived at the U.S. Consulate. The photo above shows the back side of the consulate; for the actual visa interview, you have to walk around the building to its main entrance. At the main entrance, students were lined up in loop after loop. Because of the pandemic in 2020, 20 Fall students had been unable to obtain visas and had spent a whole year taking online classes from China. Now that visa services in mainland China had reopened, it felt like their long-suppressed emotions had finally been released. My interview appointment was at 7:45 a.m. I arrived at the embassy at 7:30, and there were already more than 1,000 students at the entrance. The line was serpentine, and it wrapped around three large serpentine loops. The demand for visas from both the 2020 and 2021 cohorts was just enormous. The Beijing Embassy did its part and went all out: all 48 windows across two floors were open for student visa services. Before entering the embassy, you need to store your water bottle, umbrella, and electronic devices at the baggage storage area across the street. (The storage service charges a fee; if a parent is accompanying you, you can save that money.) You can only bring your interview documents into the embassy. There is a security check at the entrance, and even metal parts on your belt will be inspected. After entering, the first checkpoint is to verify your I-20 and passport information. After that, you continue lining up for fingerprint collection; all ten fingers on both hands need to be fingerprinted. Once fingerprinting is done, you reach the second checkpoint and line up for the interview. There is now crowd control assigning you to a specific interview window, so you cannot choose for yourself. If you notice that the window next to you has been issuing rejection slips nonstop, and crowd control assigns you to that window, all you can do is pray for the best. I was assigned to a window on the first floor near the wall. The visa officer was an Asian man. Since this was a student visa, I had to answer in English throughout, so that they could assess whether I was qualified to study in the U.S. The officer asked me many questions. I suspect the reason I was rejected was this: he asked who was funding my studies. I answered that I was funding them myself. Then he asked how many years I had been working. I said almost five years. After that he asked a few more perfunctory questions. I may already have failed at that point. Five years of work experience, the ability to work, a large amount of savings, and family members accompanying me—my immigration intent must have looked far too strong! Rejected on the spot! Of course, these reasons are just my own speculation. They do not tell you the reason for a rejection. He gave me the white slip shown below, and I left.


<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_50.png'></img></p>


The rejection reason was 214b. It said I had failed to prove that I had a residence outside the United States that I had no intention of abandoning. In other words, I lacked binding ties in China: career, job, school, family, and social relationships. After taking that white slip, I was completely stunned. For a moment I had no idea what to do. Was my path to studying abroad cut off just like that? Walking out of the embassy, the sun was bright and scorching, but inside me it felt like a torrential downpour. I returned to the hotel to pack my luggage, then dragged my suitcase to Capital Airport. I do not even remember how I got there. My mind was blank. I had no memory, no soul; only my body dragged itself to the airport.


<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_45.png'></img></p>


Coincidentally, that same day was the launch of the Shenzhou-12 crewed spacecraft, June 17, 2021 Beijing time. The crew consisted of astronauts Nie Haisheng, Liu Boming, and Tang Hongbo. Because of the Shenzhou-12 launch, Capital Airport imposed air traffic control. All air routes suspended passenger flights. My flight was scheduled for 11 a.m., but it did not take off until 5 p.m. That day, the whole country was celebrating the successful launch of Shenzhou-12. But I, alone, could not feel happy at all. I sat in a dark little corner of the airport, silently crying inside.


### 6. Second Attempt


After the failure of my first attempt, I returned to Shanghai and started thinking about what to do for the second attempt. It was already almost July. There were only eight weeks left before school started. I read many rejection posts on the forum, but very few were similar to my case. I also could not find a good breakthrough point. I had to find a way to address the “lack of binding ties” issue before the second interview could work. Because when you apply for another interview, the system asks you to fill in what is different between this application and your previous one. If you submit exactly the same materials as last time, your second attempt will be rejected directly.


<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_47.jpg'></img></p>

For this second attempt, I did not apply with a dependent and did not bring my partner with me. I planned to go alone and use my partner as one of my binding ties in China. I also changed the funding source for my studies to my parents. Fortunately, I still had bank transfer records from four months earlier. This helped rebut the implication that I was bringing a large amount of personal funds in preparation for immigration. Another binding tie was being an only child plus property ownership. I also stated in my explanation that I would return to China immediately after finishing my studies. (It seems like everyone writes this 😂) I lost the first battle at Anjialou; for the second battle, I went to Meilong Town. The Shanghai consulate is located in Meilong Town. On the day of the interview, my partner accompanied me to the building downstairs. She held my bag for me, saving me the baggage storage fee. The process was exactly the same as at the Beijing Embassy. While lining up for the interview, I kept observing the visa issuance rates at several nearby windows. The two people in front of me in my line were both put under check: one girl in biological sciences and one boy in history. Then it was my turn. The officer first asked a few brief personal-information questions. Then he asked me to wait. He was reviewing the reason why I had been rejected in my previous interview. He spent more than five minutes reading. It looked like the previous visa officer had written a lot of “bad things” about me. The waiting was truly agonizing. I almost thought I was going to fail before the interview even started. This visa officer was American. After reading all the “bad things” about me, he began the interview. Every question targeted issues from what the previous officer had written. I answered very carefully. He asked why I lived in Shanghai but went to Beijing for my previous visa interview; why two people came last time but only one person came this time; how I could prove that the funds were not my own but provided by my parents. The questioning went on for a full hour. My mouth was dry from answering. The final result was a yellow slip: check! He told me to go back and wait, with no additional documents required. I felt like I was going to be rejected again. My mindset collapsed on the spot. When I walked away from the interview window, there was nobody behind me anymore. Because my interview had taken too long, crowd control had assigned everyone behind me to other windows. I took the yellow slip and went downstairs. I found my partner. She was extremely anxious and asked why the interview had taken so long. My appointment was at 7:30. By the time I came out, it was already 9:30. From my expression, she could tell the result was bad. Being put under check was actually a very bad outcome. If I had been rejected again on the spot, I could still schedule a third attempt. But now it was July. Once put under check, it usually takes eight weeks to get a result. If I were rejected after eight weeks, I would no longer have a chance for a third attempt. School would have already started by then. Whether I could go to the U.S. to study now depended entirely on the outcome of this second attempt. If it failed, this experience-sharing article might never have existed. I was put under Check on June 29 for my second attempt. It was not updated to Administrative Processing until August 12.

Fortunately, it was Issued right after that on August 13. By the time I received my passport with the visa, it was already August 18. That week, I hurriedly said goodbye to some close friends. The following Monday, the 23rd, I flew Guangzhou - Seoul - San Francisco. If you are also applying for a U.S. visa, the only advice I can give is: good luck! If you really get rejected, be patient and try a few more times. There is still a chance you can pass.


### 7. Exit-Entry Customs Medical Exam and Competing for an Appointment

Before flying out of China, you need to go to Exit-Entry Customs to obtain an international health certificate, commonly known as the yellow booklet and the red booklet.

<p align='center'><img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/154_46.png'></img></p>

Because the pandemic in 2020 disrupted the entire world, everyone became extremely sensitive about COVID-19 vaccination. Without vaccination, or without a negative nucleic acid test result, you cannot board the plane. It was extremely difficult to book an entry-exit medical exam with Shanghai Customs! At the end of July, I tried to book a medical exam for August. I could not get an appointment at all; everything was fully booked. Later, I learned in a group chat that other students had booked theirs as early as April, three months ahead of me. No wonder I could not get a slot! So I recommend that students planning to study abroad book this medical exam as early as possible!! Otherwise, you may not be able to get an appointment. Seeing that Shanghai had no available slots, I looked for medical exams in nearby cities. I first checked Nanjing and Suzhou, but unfortunately, Nanjing suddenly had a COVID outbreak. If I went to Nanjing for the medical exam, I might not be able to return to Shanghai afterward. So I checked the Exit-Entry Administration Center in Hangzhou. I successfully booked an entry-exit medical exam in Hangzhou. The medical exam has two parts. One part is the basic physical exam, and the other part is vaccination. The vaccines are required by the school, such as the meningococcal vaccine, varicella vaccine, and so on. Each school is different, and the required vaccines are also different. Some vaccines are not available in China because the corresponding diseases do not exist in China and only exist in the U.S. So some vaccines need to be taken after entering the U.S. Of course, you can also choose to take all of them after entering the U.S. I completed the medical exam in China and also received the school-required vaccines in China.


> If you want to save money, I recommend getting the school-required vaccines after entering the U.S. That is because each school’s health insurance can fully reimburse this part of the vaccine cost. If you get vaccinated in China, you pay entirely out of pocket.


### 8. Getting the COVID Vaccine

You can get the COVID vaccine either in China or in the U.S. Many students worry: if they get two vaccine doses in China and then get U.S. vaccines after arriving in the U.S., will there be any conflict? I have already completed two doses of the Pfizer vaccine in the U.S., and I had also completed two doses of a Chinese vaccine. Everything is normal so far. In China, I received the Sinovac vaccine. My first dose was on April 10, and my second dose was on May 15. After a six-month gap, I got my first Pfizer dose in California at the end of October, and then my second dose on November 29. I have now received all four doses, and everything is normal physically. As for students who are still hesitating about whether to get vaccinated in China, or whether they can receive a U.S. COVID vaccine immediately after entering the U.S., my answer is: I recommend getting vaccinated in China first. During international transfers, there are many infection risks. Getting vaccinated adds an extra layer of protection for yourself.


### 9. Wisdom Tooth Extraction + Glasses + Nucleic Acid Test

Tooth extraction is also very important. If you need to have your wisdom teeth removed, it is best to do it in China. In the U.S., tooth extraction requires appointments, the overall timeline is long, and it costs a lot of money. I had two wisdom teeth that had not been removed. Extracting them required a bone-cutting saw, and only one hospital in all of Shanghai had that tool, so an appointment was required. After the extraction, recovery would take another three weeks, during which I could not receive any other vaccines. I remembered the wisdom tooth issue a bit too late—only in August. So I had to choose: either remove the wisdom teeth and skip the school-required vaccines, or get the school-required vaccines and not remove the wisdom teeth. I chose the latter. Since wisdom tooth extraction might not recover fully within three weeks, and school was about to start, I decided not to create more trouble. This also shows that tooth extraction should be planned as early as possible. Around March or April, while waiting for offers, just go and remove any teeth that need to be removed.


If you are nearsighted, I recommend getting a pair of glasses in China. In the U.S., getting glasses requires an eye exam first, followed by ordering the glasses. The whole process is long. It is better to obediently get a new pair of glasses in China and bring them as a backup.


There is not much to say about the nucleic acid test: schedule it two days before your flight. If you need to transit through other countries, carefully check each country’s pandemic-control policies. Some countries only allow transit within 72 hours but do not allow you to enter the country. In that case, your connecting flight must be checked through directly; you cannot enter the country and then switch flights. You need to read all these details carefully yourself.

## Closing

This final section was added later because I saw a discussion on the forum about studying abroad:

1. Studying abroad during senior year is the standard path.
2. Within 1–5 years after graduating with a bachelor’s/master’s/PhD, if you have obtained household registration in a first-tier Chinese city such as Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, or Shenzhen and own at least one property, then going abroad for work/study is elite-tier behavior: a life winner experiencing life.
3. Within 1–5 years after graduating with a bachelor’s/master’s/PhD, if you have no household registration and no property in a first-tier Chinese city such as Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, or Shenzhen, then going abroad for work/study makes you a loser.

There really are people around me who fit the second category. After finishing a master’s degree, they directly obtained Shanghai household registration. After working for 3–5 years, from the third to fifth year after the master’s, they bought a property in Shanghai in full. That is truly impressive. Some readers may wonder: does it have to be paid in full? Housing prices in Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou are too high; buying in full must rely on parents. The forum discussion actually also included buying with a mortgage. But if you buy a home in Beijing or Shanghai and then go abroad to study, you cannot work while studying, so you have no monthly income. Your monthly mortgage payments can only be supported by your previous savings or your parents, on top of monthly rent and living expenses in the U.S. The total cost is not small. People or families who can support this level of spending can already be considered elite in China.

As for me, if I match myself against the categories, I am in category three. I am the loser.
