# Opening

As technologists, by the end of the year we usually do some self-reflection or write a summary, looking back to see how much we have grown over the past year. I am no exception, and I also plan to start recording my year-end summaries from 2017 onward. Although this kind of retrospective is not strictly a technical article, I wanted to avoid turning it into a plain diary, so I racked my brain for a more novel way to present it to readers. I decided to use a question that many people care about as the thread running through the entire piece. Barring surprises, I will use this format every year going forward: one broad, macro-level question per year. The experiences in these articles are guaranteed to be 100% firsthand—some successful cases, some failed ones. Of course, each reader’s situation will differ from mine, so you can take what is useful and adapt it to your own circumstances. Some viewpoints in this article represent only my personal opinions; if anything seems inappropriate, feel free to bring it up for discussion and critique.

---------------------------------------------

My Chinese pen name online is “A Wisp of Sorrow, Flowing into Hidden Half-Frost,” often shortened by others to “Frost,” “Frost Veggie,” or simply “Frost.” Since this once-a-year annual article is meant to be special, I wanted to give the series a special name as well. In Chinese culture, four-character idioms tend to sound more meaningful, and the idiom also needed to contain the character “Frost” and convey the sense of passing time. After a lot of searching, I settled on the title of this series — The Years Drift By.


>【Explanation】Stars and Frost: the stars complete one cycle each year, and frost begins to fall every autumn; by extension, it refers to the passage of years. It means that time gradually slips away.
>
>【Source】Wen Tingyun of the Tang dynasty, “Sending to Mr. Cui”: “The years drift by with no word from you; amid misty waters, names have changed.”
