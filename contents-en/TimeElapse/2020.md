2020 was undoubtedly one of the most historically significant years in human history. That year saw a severe global public health crisis. Wuhan was at the center of the storm, and also at the center of public opinion. Conspiracy theories appeared one after another. Fortunately, under the leadership of the Communist Party, Wuhan brought its original confirmed cases down to dynamic zero in roughly a little over three months. Also because of those three months of lockdown, Wuhan’s economy was hit hard. My parents’ restaurant suffered a devastating blow and eventually shut down. Our family’s finances were also badly affected.


On January 23 this year, after Grandpa Zhong Nanshan announced that the novel coronavirus could spread from person to person, I canceled my high-speed rail ticket and did not go home. Although I was in Shanghai, my heart was basically still with Wuhan. Every few hours, the news apps would push updates on the pandemic situation, and each update tugged at my heart. A dozen new cases here today, a dozen new cases there tomorrow—it made people constantly anxious. I still remember going to the pharmacy to buy masks. On a rainy day, everyone queued in a very, very long line, and each person could buy only two N95 masks per day. In the early days of the outbreak, masks were as precious as life itself; without a mask, you simply did not dare go outside. And at that time masks were in extremely short supply. Every day at home, I counted masks to get by. When masks were about to run out, I had to hurry out, walk several streets, and queue up to buy more. Netizens posted heartwarming comics online, temporarily easing the tension.

Another thing that left a deep impression on me was that China once again proved its reputation as an infrastructure-building powerhouse. In just a few days, the two hospitals, Leishenshan and Huoshenshan, were built.

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/151_6.jpg)


In fact, the names Huoshen and Leishen have their origins. In the legends of Chu culture, Hubei was the ancient land of Chu, and the people of Chu were believed to be descendants of the fire god Zhurong, while Zhurong (Emperor Ku) was a descendant of the Yellow Emperor. **In the Five Elements, the human lungs correspond to metal, and fire overcomes metal**. The novel coronavirus, which harms human lungs, is afraid of high temperatures, and the fire god can drive away the plague god. Thus the name “Huoshenshan” came into being.

As for Leishenshan, it also corresponds to a legend in Chinese folk culture: the thunder god is the god who punishes evil. Anyone who violates human ethics and law and commits unforgivable crimes will be struck dead by five thunderbolts. This is the origin of the name “Leishenshan.” Today, China as a whole is strengthening its confidence, standing together through thick and thin, practicing scientific prevention and control, and implementing precise policies. A people’s war to prevent and control the novel coronavirus is being won. With Huoshenshan and Leishenshan now “joining the fight,” people’s spiritual strength has become even stronger!


Led by China Construction Third Engineering Bureau, with three companies including Wuhan Construction Engineering, Wuhan Municipal Engineering, and Hanyang Municipal Engineering participating, Wuhan Caidian Huoshenshan Hospital was built to provide centralized treatment for patients with novel pneumonia. It was called Wuhan’s “Xiaotangshan Hospital” and was scheduled to be completed before February 3. On Chinese New Year’s Eve, the Huoshenshan Hospital construction site was brightly lit, with all kinds of machinery running at full power. This was a tough battle, and the builders raced against time, striving to finish as early as possible. Netizens all switched into “cloud supervisor” mode. More than 50 million netizens watched the construction of Huoshenshan and Leishenshan from home.

## After Resigning

After the pandemic in 2020, the trajectory of my life also suffered an irreversible “blow.” Many things could no longer return to how they were, and it was impossible for them to do so. After receiving my year-end bonus in June, I chose to resign and do some things I immediately wanted to do. After leaving my job, I did not do anything earth-shattering; I just traveled around the world a bit and relaxed. (I won’t go into the stories in this part. Since it was during the pandemic, talking about travel feels somewhat against China’s pandemic-prevention spirit—do not travel unless necessary.) Money is indeed very important, but compared with time, it does not seem quite as important. Some readers may disagree with me on this, and that is fine. After all, everyone’s experiences are different, and so are their understandings of certain things. The pandemic also gave me a deeper understanding of the following “life philosophies”:


1. You can never finish earning money in this life. After you earn 1 million, you immediately start thinking about earning 5 million. After you earn 5 million, you start thinking about earning 100 million, or 1 billion. Of course, the more money, the better. So on the road to making money, there is never an endpoint. Then what is the purpose of making money?
2. For me, making money is meant to help me realize some life ideals. I know very well that I was not born like Steve Jobs to change the world. The ideal of my life is an ideal life. For ordinary people, the purpose of making money is generally the same: to improve their own lives, give the next generation a better life, improve their parents’ quality of life, and so on. Put in a more lofty way, it is to move up one’s social class. My parents’ generation lived through the Cultural Revolution. For that generation, even studying was a luxury; every day they were busy “fighting landlords.” (The mathematician Chen Jingrun was truly an “oddity” of that era.) So the historical mission of my parents’ generation was to let their children—that is, my generation—attend a good university and not have to worry about education. My parents can be considered to have fulfilled their wish: I went to university (though not a top university). Then what is the historical mission of my generation? One generation must surely be stronger than the last. My next generation should at least have a master’s degree or a PhD. (Just kidding.) From the perspective of social class, my parents’ generation was one that came through hardship. They wanted to study but had no books to read; most of them only had high-school diplomas. Later, as the country pursued large-scale production campaigns, industry was revitalized. At that time, my parents were not yet even modestly well-off. Their historical mission was to move my generation up one class: into the middle class. Then the historical mission of my generation is at least to let my child be born into the middle class, and then do everything I can to let him enjoy better education and healthcare, and help him realize his life ideals. After saying all of this, the purpose of making money becomes clear. I make money to fulfill my historical mission.
3. Since the road of making money has no end, are there any milestones along the way? For example, if the goal is to first earn 100 million, can first earning 5 million be considered a life milestone? I think it can. For example, when you first earn 5 million, you can first go realize some wishes. Because time waits for no one. If some wishes cannot be realized within a certain time window, they may never be realized in this lifetime. Some people may still not believe this after reading this far. Then I will casually give two examples. If a middle-class person has not traveled around the world once before age 30, the chance that they will do so later in life is not very high. Many people may say, “This is not my life ideal, so I do not need to realize it.” Fine, then let’s use another one. If you fail to fulfill some of your parents’ wishes before they are 50–60 years old, you may never be able to do so later, and it can only become an eternal regret. At that point, time waits for no one. It does not wait for you to see whether you have earned 100 million. When the time comes, it mercilessly deprives you of the right to fulfill that wish. For example, if your parents want to climb the Great Wall in this life, or go see the Potala Palace, once they get older, they may no longer be able to go to high altitudes because their bodies will not allow it. So what should one do? I chose to realize some wishes first at the life milestone of age 30. This is also why you see me resigning to fulfill some life wishes. Before age 30 is a life milestone. Because at that point, you have some savings, perhaps no children yet, and not too many burdens. At that time, you can take your parents to Lhasa, or to Iceland to see the aurora, repaying them for raising you in this life and also helping them realize some ideals they considered “extravagant wishes.” After age 30, you have a family and some career responsibilities, and you may be busy all the way until age 40.
4. Since money can never be fully earned, what is the most valuable thing in your life? Health. A healthy body is your priceless wealth! I had not understood this issue so deeply before. Making money is important, but caring for yourself and protecting your health is even more important. In the face of this pandemic, money is so powerless. At least judging by current science and technology, money cannot buy life. In ancient times, Qin Shi Huang spent his entire life seeking an elixir of immortality; today, wealthy people study the secrets of eternal life. Rich people all hope to live forever, spending money to extend their lives. But at least for now, money cannot make people immortal. Even if it is achieved someday in the future, it will be very expensive, and not something ordinary middle-class people can do. When “poor people” face life and death, if they can bring out the trump card of health, then health is the most priceless treasure of the “poor.”


![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/151_11.jpeg)

From 2019 through December 2020, countless friends around me changed jobs, joining big companies at home and abroad as well as companies of all sizes. After moving to a good company, they would start posting their new company’s recruiting messages and various benefits on WeChat Moments, and the comments below were uniformly sour lemons 🍋. They would also DM me or @ me in groups, asking why I still had not changed jobs and when I would look for opportunities. Every time, I humbly replied, “My technical skills are too weak; I can’t pass your company’s interview,” or “I’m such trash; your company wouldn’t want me~.” In reality, I just wanted to hide my small dream. Many headhunters contacted me and asked me to look at opportunities: the job-hopping season before the New Year in January, the job-hopping season after the New Year in February and March, the post-fiscal-year job-hopping season in May, the autumn job-hopping season in September and October, and the year-end job-hopping season in November and December. Over the course of a year, only April, July, and August had slightly fewer calls. Some headhunters deleted me as a contact. Some cursed me as a dumbass, saying that with organizational adjustments happening and I still did not resign. Some called my technical skills garbage, saying that after two years as P6, I was hopeless in this life. I saw every word you said. In my heart, I also reminded myself countless times: “You really are a dumbass. Is this goal of studying abroad really worth it? What a stubborn idiot!” “My technical skills really are weak. I only know the surface of frontend, client-side, and backend, and to this day I am not proficient in anything.”


The headhunters in other people’s WeChat probably sound like this: “You’re amazing. With your technical strength, you can definitely pass the interview for Alibaba P13/Baidu T11/Tencent level 18/ByteDance 5-3. Come give it a try—I’ve already submitted your résumé for you.” But the headhunters in my WeChat had a completely different style: “You big dumbass, why haven’t you changed jobs yet? If you don’t change jobs this year, you’ll be finished for life!! You’re already finished~” (I am not exaggerating at all. Don’t say it’s impossible; you just haven’t encountered it.) Then I turned around wanting to explain, but he had already deleted me. We did not even know each other, and after only a few lines of conversation, how did my life suddenly become ruined??? I had been quietly and carefully planning my future in my heart—five-year plans, ten-year plans—and just because I did not change jobs, I was finished??? There were also “kind-hearted” headhunters who planned things for me: “This year let’s jump to ByteDance, next year let’s jump again to Tencent, and the year after that let’s jump again to Baidu. In just three years, your career will reach a certain height!” This plan differed from the plan in my heart. I said, “I have my own plan. I’m not changing jobs for now this year.” The headhunter would then ask, “What is your plan?” I said it was confidential. Then the headhunter exploded and started attacking me for being hypocritical: “If your technical skills are too garbage to jump, fine, but you insist on making up an excuse that you’re not changing jobs. I’m being so sincere with you—how can you be so hypocritical? There’s something wrong with your character. Trash~” I felt extremely wronged. Why did it turn into insults? Ten minutes later, after calming down, I wanted to explain the reason to him, but the other person had already deleted me. In 2020, I was deleted by many headhunters with “somewhat bad tempers,” and they inexplicably labeled me in all kinds of ways: “dumbass,” “no IQ,” “bad character,” “hypocritical,” “no plan,” “technical trash,” “this person is finished”... Seeing those words made me truly furious. Not a single thing they said was correct. Since I had already been deleted, I no longer had the right or opportunity to explain. Being cursed at again and again silently encouraged me in my heart: “I have to work hard! Today, in your eyes, I am a big dumbass and stinking trash, spurned and looked down upon by you. Tomorrow, I will become a hot commodity and a warm treasure in the eyes of other headhunters, making you regret it when it is too late.” And just like that, with one breath of resolve, I sustained myself for an entire year.
At first, I told some friends around me that I was studying English. Gradually, they forgot about it. People would often ask me, “Why haven’t you updated your blog?” “What have you been busy with lately?” If I answered honestly, the answer would be, “I’ve been studying astronomy and geography lately,” or “I’ve been busy preparing for the TOEFL.” Then some people would start asking again, “Why study astronomy and geography?” “Why are you taking the TOEFL? Are you going abroad? Studying overseas?” At that point, no matter what I said next, the conversation would become extremely awkward. If my colleagues found out, they would quietly spread it in private: “Frost is preparing to go abroad. He’s definitely going to resign. Let’s give him this quarter’s C anyway, since he’s leaving.” If my manager found out, during a one-on-one, there’s a good chance they would make me take a 3.25. If headhunters found out, they probably wouldn’t recommend good opportunities to me anymore. During the first few weeks of preparing for the TOEFL, I wished everyone in the group knew I had gone off to study English, and I welcomed people to discuss it with me. But after I thought through the consequences of saying it out loud, I shut up. From then on, whenever friends, colleagues, or headhunters asked me, “What have you been busy with lately?” “What’s your career plan going forward?” “When are you changing jobs?” or similar questions, I just dodged them: “Huh? Ha. What? Oh... hahaha...” Some readers may ask at this point: why did you hide it from the whole world? It’s not shameful to talk about your plan to study abroad. Then let me ask you this: before you change jobs, would you let your colleagues and manager know? Of course not. Their interests are tied to yours. It is very possible that before you even get an offer, they already know about it, and then before you have found your next job, they fire you. At that point, you would have no tears left to cry. In China, even reading the source code of an open-source library at work can make people suspect that you are about to change jobs; asking detailed questions about business logic can make people suspect that you are about to run away. And if you read technical books, technical blogs, or study at the company, then that is definitely “hard evidence” that you are job-hopping. Why should you explain your plans and intentions one by one to colleagues who already suspect you? Why create extra trouble for yourself? Studying abroad is similar to changing jobs, and you also need to hide it from friends and headhunters, because you may still need them to refer you internally. If you tell them everything, they may stop helping you with referrals. Some readers may ask again: if you are already going to study abroad, why do you still care about job referrals? That is because you don’t understand how graduate entrance exams and studying abroad work. Since we’ve come this far, let me explain it all at once. Whether it is taking the graduate entrance exam or applying to study abroad, the time when you know whether you are ultimately admitted is in the spring of the following year. For graduate admissions, the final admission list after the interview is published in April. For studying abroad, offers are issued from February to April. The graduate entrance exam is in December every year, and application deadlines for studying abroad are generally in December or January. After you finish the graduate entrance exam, or after you submit your study-abroad applications, during the period before you know whether you have ultimately been admitted, don’t you still need to keep working? You don’t need to work and can just stay at home, have fun, and live off your parents? Then you are from a wealthy family. I am from a poor family. Every month, I still have a mortgage, credit cards, Huabei, and all sorts of bills waiting for me to pay. After the graduate entrance exam in December, from January of the following year until enrollment in September, there are eight months. Of course you need to keep working and making money. After study-abroad applications end in January, until enrollment in August, there are also seven months in between. You still need to keep working hard and making money. **And you don’t even know whether you can get into the school you want. What if nobody wants you? You still need to keep working and making money**. Some people say, “You still need to make money for seven months?” In those seven months, you can make close to 500,000 RMB, enough to offset the first year of tuition abroad. Isn’t that great? You can just give up 500,000 RMB like it’s nothing? If, after you state your intention to study abroad, all your colleagues, managers, friends, and headhunters know about it, imagine how you would continue looking for a job. My circle of friends is somewhat broad. Even if the HR at the next company doesn’t know, how many days can you keep it hidden? Once word spreads in the industry, within a few days the whole world will know. What will HR think when they find out? “He’s already going to study abroad, and he’s still trying to freeload here? Fire him during probation.” Don’t laugh. You never know what is in people’s hearts. I cannot guarantee others won’t think that way. But I can guarantee that I won’t say anything. As long as I don’t say anything, I can eliminate all the bad situations that might happen. So this is also why, in 2021, you are only now seeing my 2019 year-end summary. In 2019, I started preparing for the TOEFL, and the study-abroad application phase was in the winter of 2020. If I had published my 2019 year-end summary in advance, everyone would have seen that I was preparing for the TOEFL, and people everywhere would have suspected that I was going abroad. No matter how much I tried to explain, I would not have been able to clear it up. Before I received the final offer, I definitely could not say anything. And with China-US relations deteriorating, even if I got an offer from an American university, whether my US visa would be approved smoothly was another headache. At that time, I made up my mind: the moment I set foot on American soil, I would announce all of this to everyone and publish both my 2019 and 2020 year-end summaries. **The above is why I went invisible for two years. I am very satisfied with the outcome of keeping it confidential. I basically achieved all of my goals, without interference from anyone.**


Also, I am personally conservative by nature. When the result is not 100% certain, I don’t want to reveal the process. If next year (2021), after I finish submitting my study-abroad applications in January, I start looking for a new job, then until the results come out in April, I will keep working hard as if nothing happened. If I get a good result in April, then I will resign in June. If I don’t get a good result, I can continue working. Imagine advertising to the entire world while preparing for the TOEFL: “I’m going abroad to study!” Then when your applications are over and you receive no offers at all, how awkward would that be? **Most people who talk loudly every day don’t succeed; most people who stay silent and put their heads down to work do succeed**. **This is a society that places extreme importance on results. Without outstanding results, who will care about your process?** On Zhihu, if you say you got 118 on the TOEFL in one day, a huge crowd will come watch. If you say you spent 10 years getting your TOEFL score to 100, no one will care. Your experience is a total failure and has no reference value. You and I are both tools in different people’s eyes. Others want to learn successful experience from you. In an era where winners take all and even second place gets forgotten, **effort without results ultimately only moves yourself**. This is my personality: think everything through, prepare for all exams quietly and thoroughly, then after getting the best result, come out and share the result, dropping this royal flush. In short, that is why I started keeping my mouth shut in 2019. Nobody knew what I had been busy with. Friends around me changed jobs, or deleted me as a friend. I kept my head down, took my salary, earned my bonuses, got the best offer, and finally transitioned seamlessly to studying in the US. Isn’t that great? No one could sabotage it, and I would not give anyone the chance to sabotage the “perfect” plan I had carefully buried and protected. This is probably the true meaning of “making big money quietly,” right? (manual **@大左**).

**Don’t tell anybody what your next move is. Just do it and shock them. And after shocking them, stay silent and plan your next move. And make it happen. Just keep shocking. Keep Enjoying. Keep Repeating it.**


## Preparing to Study Abroad


Even this year, perhaps many people still do not understand why I suddenly resigned, suddenly interrupted my “bright” career path, and chose to study abroad. The thought process behind this decision was already clear to me in 2019. Last year, I had already decided to take this path. But I had not clearly explained to readers why I wanted to take this path. So here, I will give the answer from the bottom of my heart:

1. Achieve my career goals 

2. Satisfy my curiosity: I want to see what the world’s top engineers are like in their day-to-day work.

3. Personal development 
4. Experience a completely new foreign culture or lifestyle 
5. Access higher-quality education 
6. Have an adventure 
7. Make new friends or expand my professional network 
8. Learn a new language (not a programming language)


The world is so big. Since we are born human, we should go out and see more of the world. Fight for the life and happiness you truly want. Once you broaden your perspective, you will truly discover that there are so many outstanding people, and that you are as tiny as a grain of sand. You will truly discover that there are many things far beyond your reach, and that you need to keep working hard. You will discover that life is more than what is right in front of you. You only live once.


I started preparing for the TOEFL in August 2019. Unfortunately, by the end of the year, I still wasn’t ready. I took it once with almost no preparation and only scored 80. The score was far from ideal. So this year, I continued preparing for the TOEFL and trying to raise my score.


![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/151_9.jpeg)


This year, I had been preparing for both the TOEFL and the GRE. But because of the pandemic, all offline test centers were closed and did not reopen until August. During those seven months, there were no exams at all. I originally thought that if I reviewed TOEFL material properly in January and February, I could take the test. Who would have known that the pandemic would suddenly break out and offline test centers would close? In the blink of an eye, it was already August. My family kept urging me to prepare for the TOEFL in advance, so that once test centers reopened, I could immediately “grab” a test slot. But I was still lazy. Deadlines are still the primary productive force. Without the constraint of a specific test date, my study efficiency was never high. In general, everyone’s study efficiency is highest during the week before an exam. Because you know you will be entering the exam room a week later, even last-minute cramming becomes extremely serious. I did not manage to book my second TOEFL exam until August 15. After finishing that TOEFL exam, I began preparing for the GRE.

> To be honest, I feel like 2020 passed way too quickly. In the blink of an eye, it was gone. Every day, I watched pandemic updates from all over the world, and wore a mask through all four seasons. I didn’t do anything, and the year was over.


As for preparing for the GRE, I do not have much successful experience to share. My final score did not reach 325 either. Generally, if your TOEFL is around 100, you can get about 315 on the GRE without much preparation. Of course, that score is completely insufficient. For me, the hardest GRE questions were the Verbal fill-in-the-blank questions. These questions have extremely high vocabulary requirements. Many GRE words are words that even those of us who have been memorizing vocabulary since childhood have never seen at all. That is because this exam is designed for native English speakers, while the TOEFL is designed for non-native English speakers. GRE reading and TOEFL reading are partly similar. For GRE Quant, you need to work through several sets of practice tests seriously. Otherwise, word problems will absolutely destroy you. There are many hidden conditions in them; if you fail to spot them, you will definitely get the question wrong. Another thing to note in GRE Quant: for some questions, if the given conditions are insufficient, you must not invent conditions in your head. For example, if the question does not state that the male-to-female ratio is 1:1, then when the question involves gender ratios, you should boldly choose “this problem cannot be solved.” The option “this problem cannot be solved” is extremely misleading, because you do not know whether the problem is unsolvable because of your own limitations, or because the problem itself lacks conditions. Since my major did not have very high requirements for GRE writing, a 3 or 3.5 was acceptable. So I did not invest too much time in GRE writing. Writing with my TOEFL writing ability, I could muddle through and get a 3. So overall, if you want a high GRE score, you should aim for a perfect score in Quant and as high a score as possible in fill-in-the-blank questions. The GRE full score is 340. Quant is 170, and the remaining Verbal section is also 170. Getting above 160 in Verbal is extremely difficult. Generally, if you can get above 150, then 325 is very achievable. Success or failure on the GRE depends on Verbal.


> The GRE and TOEFL have one thing in common: both may include an extra experimental section. Test takers do not know which section is experimental, and the experimental section does not count toward the score. Because you do not know which one is experimental, you still have to take every section seriously. The difference between the GRE and TOEFL is that the GRE’s difficulty dynamically changes based on the test taker’s performance. For example, if a candidate performs particularly poorly on the first section, then the second section will become very easy. But the score value of each question also dynamically decreases. In that case, even if you get the entire section correct, the score is still not 170. Only when you get everything right in hard mode can the score be the full 170. If a candidate performs particularly well in the first section and gets everything right, then the second section becomes harder. When you clearly feel that the difficulty has increased, congratulations: you have unlocked the high-score hard mode. Although your probability of answering correctly becomes lower, if you guess a question correctly, it is also worth more points.

> I was lucky: my experimental section was Quant. Quant is relatively easy. My final score was 169, so I still lost one point. (People on Zhihu say that if you can’t get a perfect 💯 on GRE Quant, you are not Chinese. 😭 I am not Chinese.)


GRE vocabulary can only be handled by memorizing more words yourself. First, memorize the vocabulary book *要你命 3000* five times. Otherwise, you will definitely be completely lost in the exam room and basically have to guess everything. If you memorize this book fewer than five times, you are swimming naked; ten times is a bikini; only fifteen to twenty times means you have made it ashore.

There are also a small number of math-related words to memorize. I used the Kaomanfen GRE vocabulary app. The essential math vocabulary list has only 360 words. Once you memorize these, they are completely enough. I also went through their GRE vocabulary once, both in forward order and reverse order. The benefit of memorizing words in an app is that you can do it anywhere, which is much more convenient than using a book. Waiting for a bus, lining up for the subway, standing in line for lunch—during all these fragments of time, you can review several rounds as long as you take out your phone. After deleting entertainment apps and games, there really is a lot of time for vocabulary review.

Finally, memorizing vocabulary is both a physical and mental grind, and you have to subject yourself to that torture every day. If you break the streak for a day or two, it may take a week to recover the accumulated memory you had built up.

About preparing for the GRE: as someone who has been through it, I can say a few words of encouragement about the mindset, because I went through it the same way. I believe those who come after me may run into the same problems.

Preparing for the GRE is also extremely painful. The pain of being a GREer is that after putting in the work, you may not see any payoff for an unknown period of time. But the difference between success and failure is whether you can get through that stretch. Countless students applying to study abroad have walked this road. Many people may spend several months preparing, only to find after sitting for the exam that they have not improved at all. Their mindset is on the verge of collapse—and all of this is normal. At that moment, it feels as if you are inside a dark tunnel with no light at all: aggrieved, lonely, even desperate. Sometimes you even want to give up. But please remember: if you finish this road even on your knees, it will give you the most precious asset in life—growth. When you truly reach the end of this road, walk out of the dark tunnel, and the sunlight falls on your face, only then can the corners of your mouth rise with the pride that comes from winning the study-abroad exam battle. At that moment, you will say to yourself: no regrets about studying abroad; youth is immortal!


As for the outcome of my study-abroad applications, I cannot share it in this year-end review; the results will not come out until April or May next year. In next year’s year-end review, I will summarize the whole application season.


## Finally

The first five years of my career have come to an end, all of them spent striving in Shanghai. According to my plan, the next five years will be spent striving around the world. I will unlock “places to work and study” across the globe. As for my second five-year plan: should I continue on to a PhD? Or should I apply for an MBA after working for a while? Schwarzman College at Tsinghua University, the UIUC MBA program, and others all look like good options. It is also possible that I may be unable to resist the temptation of money, get rich first, and then continue living around the world after achieving financial freedom. I will not set any flags for now; I will announce things after I have actually made them happen.


Finally, as usual, I will close this year-end review with a few brief reflections.

1. Life is, by nature, a trial to be endured. The process is inevitably awkward and messy, but as an adult, no matter whether you are bleeding or sweating, as long as you do not leave the field, even if you encounter great disasters and hardships, everything is still possible in the future.
2. Laziness equals poverty—it is that direct. If you are not diligent at 20, you will not be established at 30; if you are not wealthy at 40, you will decline at 50 and depend on your children for support. **What your parents give you is called background; what you build yourself is called your kingdom. Do not pretend to work hard—the results will not act along with you.**
3. There are two kinds of suffering in life: the suffering of self-discipline, and the suffering of regret. The suffering of self-discipline is as light as a feather; the suffering of regret is as heavy as Mount Tai.
4. On the surface, competition between people is about cars, houses, and face. At its core, it is about capability, resources, and connections. In essence, it is about cognition, thinking, values, and character.
5. Do not be anxious, and absolutely do not give up. Blossoming flowers and abundant fruit both require a process.


That is all for 2020’s 【Fleeting Years】. If you have any objections or anything you would like to discuss, feel free to reach out.
