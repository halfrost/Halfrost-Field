## What Is Software Development?

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/123_15.png)

Software development is the process of requirements analysis, design, implementation, testing, and bug fixing involved in creating or maintaining applications, frameworks, or application components. Software development is the process of writing and maintaining code. More broadly, **software development is a manifestation of human thought activity**.

Rather than calling software development "bricklaying," it is better to think of it as problem-solving ability and a reflection of intelligence. What you develop is not important; what matters is the angle from which you think about problems and your ability to solve them quickly. After using programming languages across front end, back end, and client-side development, I have come to feel that being able to use a language is not much by itself; the key is how large a problem you can solve with a given language. Front end and back end both have corresponding job levels. At the same level, compensation differences across development roles are not large. A higher or lower job level is more a reflection of the strength of a person's thinking ability. Moreover, in every field and direction, reaching senior developer level is not easy. Every field has its own roadmap, and deep cultivation in any one field requires 2-3 years of focused effort. It is very difficult for anyone to stay ahead continuously and remain at the top of the pyramid.

Broadly speaking, which language you use for development is merely your debut into the industry. As you continue forward, you will encounter many other languages. How to cultivate thinking ability is what a software engineering technologist needs to focus on. The gap between a rookie and an expert lies in the accumulation of effective time. This often happens: a rookie and an expert encounter the same problem at the same time—even an unfamiliar problem—and the expert can quickly identify the essence of the issue. After solving it, the expert says the breakthrough came from their "intuition." But this intuition is precisely valuable experience, and it is what rookies need time to accumulate. This "intuition" is not mysticism; it is an ability—the ability to solve problems quickly after gaining rich experience.

After going through development iterations across all three ends, I find that client-side, front-end, and back-end development processes and work content have commonalities.

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/123_16.png)

The development process is consistent across all three: review, scheduling, kickoff, stand-ups, development, finalization, testing, gray release, and production release.

Each end has APM and needs performance monitoring, though the architectural implementations differ. The concerns of the three ends are different. Client-side and front-end development focus more on customers, user experience, page load speed, and so on. Server-side development focuses primarily on services: service performance, availability, high concurrency, low latency, IO read/write speed, active-active deployments, cross–data center deployment, and so on. Some readers may bring up the issue of a "contempt chain" here, but I think there is no need to look down on other ends. Developers who do pure server-side work are often not very sensitive to graphics and pixels; asking them to implement front-end animations may be difficult. Developers who do pure front-end work may not be familiar with back-end architecture; asking them to design large-scale high-concurrency systems may be difficult. (Considering that some readers are full-stack developers and understand all three ends very well, I deliberately added the word "pure" here.) Asking pure server-side developers to write front end may not necessarily work; asking pure client-side developers to write server-side systems may also not necessarily work. Each end has its own difficulties. We can learn from one another, but there is no need for contempt.

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/123_17.png)

In summary, in the narrow sense, software development is the process from implementing requirements to final production release. In the broad sense, it is the process of solidifying and crystallizing human thought activity into software products. The process of software development continuously trains people's thinking and their ability to discover and solve problems.

## Embrace Change

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/123_1.png)

Over the past year, the question I was asked most often was: "Your company was acquired—does that mean XXX is going to happen?" XXX refers to many things friends around me speculated about, such as mergers, layoffs, N+1, resignations, and so on. From January 2018 to the end of February, when the acquisition by Alibaba was announced, a great many things indeed happened inside the company, and the organizational structure changed significantly. After the acquisition, internal changes were also substantial. Of course, this can be considered standard procedure; after all, there are many resource-integration processes after an acquisition. Some people and things closely related to me changed dramatically. Perhaps because of normal post–Chinese New Year turnover, within a month many company notes for colleagues in my WeChat contacts changed. Close "comrades-in-arms" who had worked with me late into the night left me. A teammate who was still discussing problems with me at a bus stop in the middle of the night would wake up the next day and tell me they were leaving. I am someone who values relationships deeply. After all, we were a team; we had fought our way through together, and the ironclad bonds forged through "life and death" experiences suddenly ended in a "breakup." It really did not feel good. After learning the reasons for their departure, I could only sigh and wish them great success at their new company. The atmosphere around me during that period was truly oppressive, so I went to Japan and tried hard to let everything go, following the direction in which the cherry blossoms were blooming and soaking in hot springs along the way. Of course, the more you want to forget something, the more deeply it becomes etched in memory. Now, at the end of 2018, looking back on that time, the only impact on me was emotional, between friends. Other impacts were basically nonexistent. Organizational changes affected the upper levels the most—the foreman layer. They had little impact on bottom-level bricklayers and cement workers like us. If I am not just honestly laying bricks, what else am I thinking about?

There were also many changes in my own technical work. At the beginning of the year, I planned to participate in an artificial intelligence–related project. So I read the Watermelon Book and watched some videos to get started with AI. Most introductory knowledge was relatively easy to understand; it was all about advanced mathematics, linear algebra, and probability theory. I had reviewed all of this carefully while preparing for graduate school entrance exams, so I had not forgotten much and picked it back up quickly. Later, due to some organizational changes, I was unable to participate in that project. The hot technologies in 2017 were artificial intelligence (AI) and blockchain. At the beginning of the year, I also read four introductory blockchain books on my Kindle. The underlying technologies of blockchain are not entirely new; what is relatively novel is its design and philosophy. After reading several introductory blockchain books, I became interested in the underlying cryptographic technologies, so I picked cryptography back up and read three technical books related to it. Although I did not join the blockchain industry, cryptography is foundational knowledge in computer science and can be extended to many fields. Today, people have increasingly high requirements for security; network security and information security are all related to encryption. After joining a new project team in May, I set my KPI for the second half of the year, and my goals for that period became relatively aligned and clear. I participated in a network-related project where performance was the top priority, and network latency was also the focus of my optimization work. If HTTPS is slow, where might it be slow? Why is it slow? What does it have to do with the choice of encryption cipher suite? Why can TLS 1.3 shorten the total request time? Why does QUIC work well in weak network environments? How do cellular networks choose base stations? What is signal fallback in cellular networks? I used to have only vague answers to these questions. After heavily participating in this project and practicing in real scenarios, I now have very deep answers to all of them. After several rounds of optimization, the phased results were also recognized by the business side.

For a summary of my learning and work in 2018, see the Archive list on my blog. Looking back, the year's gains were just so-so overall, but the gains in networking were significant. In a year full of change, I embraced change.

## Career Planning and the Pursuits of Technologists

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/123_3.png)

On the topic of career, I was once confused as well, and I consulted quite a few experts about how they planned their careers. I am no longer confused now.

To clarify your own career plan, you first need to understand what your pursuit is as a technologist. Once you know where your heart is headed and your goals are clear, defining a career plan becomes very simple.

A newly graduated student who has just entered the software development industry will inevitably feel somewhat lost, not knowing what they want or how to take the road ahead. A master once gave me this advice: "In the first five years after graduation (five years at most), I recommend trying as many development directions as possible to find the direction you are truly interested in. Once you find it, bury your head and dig into it for 3-5 years." This approach may be effective for students who feel lost. For new graduates who plan to join big companies right after graduation, my advice is: your primary skill must be specialized and strong. Your primary skill is the ticket into a big company. If that skill is not specialized enough, even 10 additional skills will not help. Joining a big company is only the first step; subsequent development depends on personal planning. While doing your job well, spend your spare time studying the directions that interest you.

>On the definition of interest:  
>If you are merely following the crowd and learning the latest technologies, that is not real interest. I think true interest means being deeply obsessed. Some people forget to sleep while playing games—that is obsession with games. Obsession with technology may manifest as staying up all night reading books and writing code and forgetting to sleep (of course, I do not advocate not sleeping).

After finding the direction you are truly interested in, you can begin working hard to become TOP. Becoming top-tier in the field you are proficient in is the highest priority. In big companies, promotion evaluates your professionalism. No matter how broad your technical surface area is, if your depth does not meet the requirements of the next level, you cannot be promoted. Take me as an example: my mobile-side skill score might be just a bit above passing; I know a little about front end and back end, perhaps 30 points each. In this situation, big companies might not even want me. Do not expect a big company to hire you to do the work of three people alone, because big companies are fully capable of hiring three people who each score 90 in their respective fields. In that case, being full-stack does not have much advantage. Promotion in big companies still relies more on professional depth.

However, in your spare time, you can look at other languages and absorb their respective strengths. Before I came into contact with Go, I did not have much feeling for coroutines; I had only heard of them, and I knew almost nothing about how coroutines are scheduled in user space. Why did Go design goroutines this way? What problem was it trying to solve? Was Go the first to implement coroutines? Who was the first to implement coroutines? Can Swift implement coroutines? If not, why not? Can OC implement them? How? These are all questions worth studying and thinking about in ordinary times. Breadth of perspective can deepen your understanding of development and improve your understanding of the industry.

Let us also talk about the pursuits of technologists. As a technologist, my pursuit should be to spend my life cultivating a "signature technique" that becomes famous throughout the world—a representative work that becomes the name by which I am known. Wandering the world, knowing many weapons and countless martial arts, or being able to use many programming languages, is not legendary in itself. What matters is whether you can use your strongest weapon to kill with one strike. Ten years of sharpening a sword, with unique craftsmanship, just to give an account of this technical life. I am also taking this pursuit as my goal for the next few years and working hard to realize it.

If a technologist's pursuit is to make money quickly and realize the dream of financial freedom, then one more point must be taken seriously: pigs standing in the wind really do get lifted up. Quickly choosing the direction of the next big trend is more important than hard work. I saw too many such things around me in 2018, and Lei Jun's entrepreneurial memoirs also mention this point. **Moving forward with effort is certainly important, but choice can sometimes be even more important**!

Many people have asked me similar questions: "Shuang, you switched to back end—doesn't that waste the three years of client-side experience you had before?" "Shuang, after changing directions, you may not be promoted for years. Does that bother you?" "Shuang, do you regret getting into back-end knowledge?" My answer is basically always "No." I am still young, and there are indeed many opportunities for trial and error. Some "mistakes" may only become clear when I am older and look back.

When you are young, you have plenty of time, plenty of opportunities, plenty of directions, and at the same time plenty of room for trial and error. In fact, there is no path to choose at all; the heart is the compass. With fog everywhere, you can only look forward.

On the issue of changing technical direction, I feel I have some authority to speak, since I have already experienced it myself. As someone who has been through it, here is some advice for readers:

- Unless absolutely necessary, do not change direction. In a new direction, you are a newcomer, and everything starts from scratch. After changing direction, both promotion and job hopping become more troublesome. I have already come to understand this issue deeply!

- If you change jobs in order to change direction, do not accept a salary cut or demotion. With the same salary, the lower the job level, the better. This point has already been verified by colleagues around me who changed jobs and directions.

- After changing direction, you need to adjust your mindset. In your original direction you may have been treated like a big shot, but in the new direction, be at ease being a junior for two years first.

- In the new direction, give yourself confidence. Confidence is more important than gold. Even if the industry is in a winter, you must keep exercising; health is the capital of the revolution. You need to plan your time well in the new direction.

- If after changing direction your job level can still increase, please be mentally prepared and remember: **Uneasy lies the head that wears a crown**! Meet the challenge.

- Following the trend may be more important than hard work.

## The State of the Industry in 2018 and My Observations

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/123_4.png)

At the beginning of 2018, Alibaba acquired Ele.me. In the middle of the year, it merged Ele.me with Koubei. At the end of 2018, some small companies in the industry went bankrupt, while big companies also began conducting performance-based layoffs, checking attendance, and implementing 996. The image below shows some layoff information circulating online. Of course, whether it is true or false is uncertain, but it reflects some industry trends from the side.

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/123_14.png)

As an industry gradually matures, the number of people it needs should stabilize, and the bubbles of previous years will no longer appear. Hiring requirements for programmers will become higher and higher, because companies need to select the most suitable and outstanding candidates from the pool. How technical people improve both their soft and hard skills becomes especially important. In this section, I want to talk about some phenomena and thoughts I observed this year; in the next section, we will talk about how to improve technically.

This year, I saw quite a few companies being acquired or merged. The following content is not aimed at any particular company, so readers should not over-identify with it.

Company A is acquired by Company B. Company B needs to integrate Company A's resources, and unnecessary developers need to be adjusted (laid off). If you were the boss, how would you conduct layoffs? The easiest thing to think of is to lay off those with poor performance first—eliminate the bottom performers. What if it is resource integration? How do you integrate? The easiest thing to think of is to keep the best portion of duplicated resources, and lay off or transfer the rest to other departments. If the middle platform is bloated, then adjust the middle platform first.

After a small company is acquired by a large company, the greatest impact is on the small company's infrastructure. The small company's infrastructure may work passably in its own business scenarios, but once acquired, any infrastructure that is not as good as what the large company has built faces the risk of being cut. This is not to deny the capabilities of people at small companies, but rather that staffing and resources cannot compare with those of large companies. For example, for project C, the large company invests four experts for three years. A small company building the same functionality might have one developer work on it for one year. There is simply no comparability in terms of personnel investment. Then after the acquisition, project C is cut, and the relevant developers are assigned to other projects. If the other projects are ones they like, that is fine. If they are not, and the developer is personally very strong, they may consider leaving. I saw many examples like this in 2018.

The least affected are business teams. When a large company acquires a small company, it must be because the large company has not covered the acquired company's business scenarios, or has not captured a large market share. It is impossible for a large company to instantly replace the small company's business in a short time. Even if the large company rewrites the entire business, with full understanding of the business scenarios and requirements, it still requires a great deal of time, manpower, and resources. Meanwhile, the small company continues iterating its business. If the large company starts from 0 and writes a new system, it still has to keep chasing the new requirements on the small company's side. Whether such a completely disruptive rewrite has a sufficiently high OKR still needs evaluation. From this perspective, if the acquired small company's business is relatively stable, then the corresponding core developers are basically unaffected after the acquisition (note: core developers). By comparison, core infrastructure developers need to face choices such as transferring to other "peripheral" departments or departments they do not like, or moving to a business team to write business logic. The core competitiveness of core developers on a business team is that they understand the business system better than anyone else and have long since internalized the business scenarios.

Combining this with the current state of the industry, I will share some of my own views:

If you want to become the kind of highly sought-after person that big companies compete to "poach," based on my observations there are mainly two types. One type is someone who pushes a field to the extreme—95, or even 99 or 100 points. When people mention them, they represent that field or direction. They are the leader and leading figure in that field. Becoming such a person is very difficult. The corresponding title for this kind of person is XXX Senior Technical Expert. The other type is someone who pushes a product to the extreme. For example: APM, quality monitoring, container scheduling, SOA, CI/CD, cloud infrastructure, and so on. Or someone who knows a certain part of the company's business inside out: core developers of IM for WeChat or DingTalk, core developers of the transaction systems for Taobao, Tmall, or JD, and so on. These people will also be competed for. At first glance, the latter type may only score 85 or 90 in terms of technical research, but they score even higher in business familiarity.

The two types of talent above are both highly sought after by large companies today. Of course, if you have achieved neither of the above two points, that does not mean you cannot enter a big company. If you prepare well for interviews and submit your résumé carefully, there are still many opportunities.

## How to Improve?

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/123_5.png)

Many people around me have also asked me this question. I do not consider myself a good role model; many people have much stronger execution than I do. So I will talk about how I improved over the past year, and where I fell short, as a negative example for everyone.

Over the past year, besides reading technical books, I also read many columns on Geek Time. I bought several columns there. The first column I bought was Chen Hao's column (@左耳朵耗子). The content in this column is indeed very rich, and quite a bit of it is material I feel I have not yet reached the level to resonate with. Chen Hao created a reader group for the column and set an entry requirement: anyone who can persist with ARTS for a year can join. Friends around me who saw the contributions on my github would ask, "Why are there so many commits?" In fact, I just wanted to persist with ARTS for a year. If people do not push themselves, they will never know how much potential they have.

However, the result was that I did not persist. I became a negative example for everyone. ARTS stands for Algorithm, Review, Technique, and Share: doing at least one leetcode algorithm problem each week, reading and commenting on at least one English technical article, learning at least one technical technique, and sharing at least one technical article with opinions and reflection. Persist with this for at least a year. In the second half of 2018, I basically managed to read several English articles every week, learn and record technical techniques, and share multiple technical articles. But I did not keep up with the leetcode algorithm problems. Aside from weekend overtime caused by my own low efficiency, the important reason may have been that my "eyes were high but my hands were low." Problems on leetcode are divided by difficulty. For easy problems, solving 1-2 and getting AC is very quick and gives no sense of achievement. For medium and hard problems, I might spend an entire afternoon submitting and get WA every time, leading to a huge sense of frustration. Gradually, amid self-doubt, I gave up and stopped persisting. I plan to pick it up again in 2019, and hope I will not have to eat my words in the 2019 year-end summary. The github repo has already been created, to force myself a bit. I hope readers are not "all ambition and no execution" like me.

If readers are still confused about how to improve and do not know what to do, then stop being confused. First calm down and persist for one year using the ARTS method. After a year, look back and see how much you have gained.

If some readers feel they have hit a bottleneck in improvement, they can examine whether their computer science fundamentals are solid enough to let them continue building higher. Computer science fundamentals determine both the height of the superstructure and the speed of progress.

For example, take blockchain technology. If someone has very solid computer science fundamentals, they will master basic blockchain technology very quickly, because the protocols and cryptographic technologies inside are not new. The design philosophy is new, but after reading through it once, they can learn it quickly. Another example is changing direction: for an iOS developer switching to the broader front-end direction, or to artificial intelligence, or to back end, how high is the switching cost? If the fundamentals are solid, then at least the computer science fundamentals transfer seamlessly. They are universal, so the switching cost is zero. Another example: a rookie and an expert read the same technical book at the same time. The expert finishes it in a morning; the rookie takes a week. Why is there such a large gap? Because when the expert reads, most of the knowledge points in the book have already been internalized. They understand what is being discussed at a glance and do not need to think; they simply keep reading and only stop to think when they encounter something they do not know. After finishing a book, there may be only 2-3 places that require thought. It is different for the rookie. Every page may have something that requires thought, or even every paragraph. For example, when reading Introduction to Algorithms, the book often derives some mathematical formulas and then says "it is obvious that..." For rookies, that conclusion is really not obvious. When a rookie reaches that line, they may think about it for an entire morning and still not necessarily understand it. The expert has derived it before or already understood the conclusion long ago, so they pass that line in two seconds. From the example of reading speed, it is easy to see where your knowledge gaps are, and everyone's gaps are different. My suggestion is that **learning should place more emphasis on returning to the source; computer science fundamentals are truly important**.

Using a change of direction as an example: suppose a student previously worked in iOS and has now switched to back end. How should they continue improving in the new direction? First, their computer science fundamentals still need to be solid: computer organization, compiler principles, computer networks, algorithms and data structures, and operating systems must all be very familiar. They are the foundation of your superstructure. The more solid the foundation, the higher the superstructure can be built.

## An Architect’s Day-to-Day Work

![](https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/123_10.png)

Over the past year, I deliberately observed what architects do in their day-to-day work. An architect’s job is to propose the most appropriate and best architecture plan for every system in the company, and to control all kinds of system metrics. Architects must have a thorough understanding of the business; otherwise, they cannot design an architecture tailored to the system. The architecture plan here must be custom-built. A small company’s business scale is not as large as that of a big company. If it adopts an architecture designed for a large company with hundreds of millions of users, that is obviously a waste of resources. Therefore, architecture plans change dramatically as business scale changes dramatically. In addition, architects must also understand system metrics. How each business in the company uses system components and consumes resources all falls within their scope of work. When I apply for machine resources, the request has to be approved by architects. They review what business it is for, what the business scenario is, and whether the request is reasonable.

Architects also need technical foresight. A good architecture continuously evolves as the business grows. A good architecture should have headroom for the current business and should also anticipate business growth, rather than failing to adapt when the business suddenly grows and triggering a cascading collapse. This kind of foresight and judgment is also a capability. This year, I ran into several technical refactorings that lacked foresight. Because I did not make good predictions in advance, sudden incidents could only be handled through emergency overtime. If I had made better predictions, I could have reduced a lot of overtime, and the system would have run more stably and evolved more smoothly.

I believe the requirements for being an architect are quite high, and the role also demands a broad perspective. If you have not seen good and reasonable architecture designs across various scenarios, relying only on untested imagination is not enough. After all, in architecture review meetings, architects are expected to provide valuable suggestions for good architecture. I tried to “point fingers” at a former colleague’s system architecture design and found that many design decisions were trade-offs based on business scenarios, not bad designs. In the end, I could not offer any good suggestions. Being an architect is indeed difficult. Without a deep understanding of different businesses and practical experience with good architecture design, there is basically no chance.


In addition, architects operate at a higher level of concern. They are not limited to any single technical point; they focus more on outcomes and returns. Why are architects paid so highly? Is it because they are familiar with every line of code in the browser kernel? (This is just an example; of course, many architects are indeed proficient in every line of the browser kernel.) Not really. Their value lies in how significant a problem they help the company solve. The high value they create for the company corresponds to their high compensation. They care more about returns. The purpose of a good architecture plan must be to solve problems. Using a particular technology is only a means. What problem can this technology solve? What is good about it? What is the return? Can it solve the problem? When I had just graduated, I paid more attention to individual technical points, and my perspective was relatively narrow. The shift from focusing only on a single technical point to focusing on the value delivered by technology can be considered a watershed in a programmer’s way of thinking.


The way architects look at problems and solve them is very much worth learning from.

Escaping the noise of the city, on an unfamiliar night, listening to frogs croaking and rain falling, I walk into my heart and look directly at my true self.

My dream for 2019 is to go to Dubai and complete a 15,000-meter skydive, and to ride a camel in the desert. My travel plans for 2019 are mainly Dubai, Europe, and the United States: to visit Germany, France, Italy, and Switzerland, and see whether the old European powers are still doing well today; to go to the United States and experience the strong academic atmosphere of Ivy League schools; to go to Dubai and see how extravagant the lives of the men in white robes are, pick up some “trash” left by the roadside—Lamborghinis—and stay in a seven-star hotel.
