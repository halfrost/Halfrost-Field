## 1. Why Do We Need Public-Key Certificates?

In the previous article, we discussed digital signatures. Digital signatures can detect tampering or whether the sender’s identity has been impersonated—that is, they verify message integrity. They can also authenticate messages and prevent repudiation. Everything seems perfect, but their weakness lies precisely in their strength: a digital signature requires a public key to confirm the sender’s identity.

We must obtain an untampered public key from a sender who has not been impersonated. To verify whether the public key we obtained is legitimate, we must use a **public-key certificate**. A certificate treats a public key as a message and has a trusted third party sign it, producing a certified public key.

## 2. What Is a Public-Key Certificate?

<p align='center'>
<img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/105_2.png'>
</p>

A public-key certificate (PKC) records personal information (name, organization, email address, and other personal details) and the person’s public key, and is digitally signed by a Certification Authority / Certifying Authority (CA). A public-key certificate is also simply called a certificate.


## 3. Public-Key Certificate Standards

<p align='center'>
<img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/105_5.png'>
</p>

The most widely used specification today is X.509 (RFC3280), defined by the ITU (International Telecommunication Union) and ISO (International Organization for Standardization). Most programs essentially follow this standard specification for certificate generation and exchange.


## 4. Public-Key Certificate Use Cases

Let’s use an example to illustrate how certificates are used. The figure below shows a scenario where Alice sends ciphertext to Bob. Bob’s public key, used to generate the ciphertext, is obtained through a certification authority.

Steps 1–7 in the figure are labeled in order. Some additional notes for specific steps:

- 1. Bob’s key may be generated by Bob himself, or it may be generated on his behalf by the certification authority.
- 3. After obtaining Bob’s public key, the certification authority begins verifying whether this public key really belongs to Bob. There are three levels of verification: Class 1 verifies the user’s identity through email; Class 2 verifies the user’s identity through a third-party database; Class 3 verifies the user’s identity through in-person verification and identity documents. The higher the class, the stricter the identity verification.
- 5. Alice uses the public key of the certification authority Trent to verify the digital signature in the certificate. If verification succeeds, she confirms that the public key contained in the certificate belongs to Bob.
- 6. Although the diagram labels this as “public-key encryption,” in practice this step can also be implemented using hybrid encryption.


Steps 1, 2, 3, and 4 in the diagram are not required every time. Steps 1, 2, and 3 only occur when registering a new public key. Step 4 is required only the first time public-key cryptography needs to be used; once the public key is saved on the computer, it does not need to be requested every time.


## 5. What Is Public-Key Infrastructure (PKI)?

Public-Key Infrastructure is the general term for a series of specifications and standards designed to make more effective use of public keys. Its abbreviation is PKI.

PKI is a general term; it does not refer to a single specific standard or specification. The PKCS (Public-Key Cryptography Standards) series of specifications developed by RSA is one type of PKI; Internet RFCs (Request for Comments) are also a type of PKI; X.509 is also a type of PKI. APIs (Application Programming Interfaces) and specification documents written by individual companies can also be considered PKI-related specifications.


>Public-Key Infrastructure (PKI) should not be misunderstood as meaning “there is only one public authority for the public” or “all public keys in the world are certified by a single root CA.” These are incorrect interpretations. A certification authority only needs to digitally sign a public key, so anyone can become a certification authority.

### 1. Components of PKI

- **Users** — people who use PKI
- **Certification authorities** — entities that issue certificates
- **Repositories** — databases that store certificates

<p align='center'>
<img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/105_8.png'>
</p>

Both users and certification authorities can be people or computers; collectively, they are called **entities**. An entity is the actor that performs certificate- and key-related operations.


There are two types of users: those who want to use PKI, and those who want to use registered public keys.

Users like Bob, who register public keys:

- Generate a key pair (either by themselves or through a certification authority)
- Register a public key with a certification authority
- Apply to the certification authority for a certificate
- Request revocation of a registered certificate
- Decrypt received ciphertext
- Digitally sign messages

Users like Alice, who use public keys:

- Encrypt messages and send them to recipients
- Verify digital signatures


### 2. Certification Authority (CA)

A Certification Authority (CA) is the entity that manages certificates. It is mainly responsible for the following operations:

- Generate key pairs (these may also be generated by users)
- Authenticate the identity of people registering public keys
- Generate and issue certificates
- Revoke certificates

A certification authority may also include a separate Registration Authority (RA). The registration authority handles registration-related work, while the certification authority focuses on issuing and revoking certificates.

### 3. Repository

A repository is a database that stores certificates. A repository is also called a certificate directory. Revoked certificates also need to be recorded in a **Certificate Revocation List** (CRL).

### 4. Certificate Chain

<p align='center'>
<img src='https://img.halfrost.com/Blog/ArticleImage/105_6.png'>
</p>

Certificates can also have a hierarchical structure: for example, a certificate issued by a top-level root CA, then certificates issued by subordinate CAs, forming a hierarchy level by level.


## 6. Attacks Against Public-Key Certificates

Because certificates are in fact based on digital signature technology, all attacks against digital signatures are also effective against certificates.

### 1. Attacking Before Public-Key Registration

A man-in-the-middle can replace a user’s public key with the attacker’s own public key when the user submits the public key to a certification authority for certification. There are two ways to prevent this kind of man-in-the-middle attack:

- When sending the public key to the CA, the user encrypts it using the CA’s public key.
- After verifying the user’s identity, the certification authority sends the fingerprint of the public key back to the user as a receipt for confirmation.

>There is an organization called a PKG (Private Key Generator), whose role is parallel to that of a certification authority (CA). It uses publicly available information—email address, mailing address, name, and other **ID-based cryptographic** information—to generate private keys. It relies on trust in the ID. The private key generator is responsible for generating a private key based on the ID and securely sending the private key to the legitimate recipient. Because the private key generator has the ability to decrypt all ciphertexts, its own security requires even greater attention.
>
>Compared with PKI, the advantage of PKG is that the sender does not need to obtain the recipient’s public key before encryption, and the recipient does not need to manage their own private key before actually performing decryption.
>

### 2. Attacking by Registering a Similar Name

An attacker can register a similar name to trick the other party into trusting them.

### 3. Attacking by Stealing the Certification Authority’s Private Key

This method attacks the CA itself. If the CA discovers that its private key has indeed been stolen, it must promptly notify all users, revoke the relevant certificates, and update the CRL.

### 4. An Attacker Impersonates a Certification Authority

A certification authority does not need to be registered; anyone can act as a certification authority. The only question is whether other CAs recognize it. An attacker can pretend to be a CA and issue certificates to users; in that case, certificate verification will naturally pass. However, the contents of the communication are no longer secret. For example, an attacker can perform DNS poisoning in an area or attack a Wi-Fi signal, causing users in that area to mistakenly believe the attacker is a trusted CA, and then proceed with the attack.

**If a certification authority is not trustworthy, then even if a certificate is valid, the corresponding public key must not be used**.


### 5. The “Loophole” in CRLs

An attacker can exploit the time gap in CRL publication to mount an attack. A sends B an email saying they need 1,000,000. After B transfers 1,000,000 to A, A repudiates the transaction and claims they never sent that email. The signature was not A’s, because the certificate had already been revoked. B did not update the CRL in time, allowing the other party to repudiate the message.

Countermeasures against this attack:

- Notify the certification authority as soon as the public key becomes invalid (user A)
- Publish the CRL as soon as possible (CA)
- Update the CRL promptly (user B)


## 7. Problems Public-Key Certificates Cannot Solve

At this point, the introduction to public-key certificates is complete. Public-key certificates currently do not have any unsolved problems. They can detect tampering or whether the sender’s identity has been impersonated—that is, they verify message integrity. They can also authenticate messages and prevent repudiation.

The difference between public-key certificates and digital signatures lies in the certification authority. Some readers may wonder why a certification authority is necessary.

- If you can obtain a trusted public key, you do not need a certification authority. This is only possible face to face with the person themselves; otherwise, it cannot be trusted.
- If you possess the public key of a trusted certification authority and trust the identity verification performed by that authority, then you can trust the certificates issued by that authority and the public keys obtained through that path.
- A certification authority **creates a trust relationship in a completely untrusted environment**.
